*A Qurʾanic study tracing the term "yaqīn" (certainty) from its linguistic root to its threefold epistemic structure and its bearing on the steadiness of the heart. By Dr. Ahmed Muhammad Ali Abouseif, President of the American Imams Academy (AIA).*

Yaqīn — that word which many suppose to be a single, indivisible state: either the heart settles into it and finds peace, or it does not settle and remains suspended between doubt and conjecture. Yet when the Qurʾan speaks of yaqīn, it does not describe a single state grasped all at once, but successive degrees, as though the road to certainty were itself a journey, not a single halt. And in this gap between the common notion of certainty and the Qurʾan's actual certainty lies most of our error in understanding a word we repeat so often without realizing it has a precise structure — indeed, the Qurʾan itself warns of a false certainty its holder mistakes for knowledge, when it is conjecture in disguise.

### First: Counting the Word in the Qurʾan, Between Noun and Verb

Let us begin where every linguistic inquiry begins: with the count. The root (y-q-n) occurs in the Noble Qurʾan in twenty-eight places, fourteen in verbal form ("yūqinūn," "yastayqinūn," "mūqinīn") and fourteen in nominal form ("al-yaqīn"), and its meaning across all these occurrences does not exceed four senses. The first and most frequent sense is "affirmation": as in "and of the Hereafter they are certain [yūqinūn]" (al-Baqarah: 4), meaning they affirm the reality of the Hereafter and the resurrection, with parallels in several places (al-Naml: 3; Luqmān: 4; al-Sajdah: 24; al-Jāthiyah: 4, 20). The second sense is "death," confined to exactly two places: "and worship your Lord until certainty [al-yaqīn] comes to you" (al-Ḥijr: 99) and "until certainty [al-yaqīn] came to us" (al-Muddaththir: 47). The third sense is "direct witnessing," in "the knowledge of certainty [ʿilm al-yaqīn]" and "the eye of certainty [ʿayn al-yaqīn]" (al-Takāthur: 5, 7), and what connects to them, "the truth of certainty [ḥaqq al-yaqīn]" (al-Wāqiʿah: 95; al-Ḥāqqah: 51). The fourth sense is pure "knowledge" as opposed to conjecture, and it has a single striking instance, mentioned below.

Yaqīn has a fifth, narrower use worth noting: "certain news" (nabaʾ yaqīn), as when the Qurʾan quotes the hoopoe informing Solomon, peace be upon him, about Sheba: "and I have come to you from Sheba with certain news [bi-nabaʾin yaqīn]" (al-Naml: 22), meaning a firm report admitting no doubt. This usage reminds us that yaqīn in the Qurʾan is not a quality confined to great matters of belief alone, but extends to every report whose verification is demanded before it is transmitted — a methodological principle by which the Qurʾan anticipated what is known today as source verification.

This very distribution carries a meaning of its own: in most of its occurrences, yaqīn is a quality sought and earned ("that he might be of those with certainty [al-mūqinīn]"), not a state a person is born already possessing — and this alone refutes the common notion of certainty as a gift either granted or withheld without effort.

### Second: The Linguistic Root, and a False Certainty the Qurʾan Warns Against

And if certainty is a quality to be sought and earned rather than a ready-made gift, it is because its very linguistic root carries the sense of motion, not initial stillness. "Yaqīn" in the language is the opposite of "doubt": al-Rāghib al-Iṣfahānī said it is "the stillness of understanding together with the fixedness of judgment," and Ibn ʿĀshūr said it is "decisive knowledge that admits no doubt." The difference between "knowledge" and "certainty" is that knowledge may be accompanied by psychological hesitation despite its correctness, whereas certainty is knowledge that has reached a degree of firmness at which not merely doubt, but anxiety itself, becomes impossible.

This is why the Qurʾan chose this very word to name death in the two verses cited above; death is the one event in which there is no room for the slightest doubt, however much a person may doubt everything else — a coerced certainty that comes to a person whether he wills it or not, as against the elective certainty of faith, which the heart pursues and is never compelled into.

But the Qurʾan does not stop here. In one remarkable verse it establishes that personal conviction alone is not called certainty unless it corresponds to the truth. God says of the killing of Jesus, peace be upon him: "They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them... and they did not kill him with certainty [yaqīnan]" (al-Nisāʾ: 157). Ibn ʿAbbās, may God be pleased with him, explained that the pronoun in "they killed him" refers to "conjecture," not to Jesus, so the meaning becomes: they did not kill their own conjecture with certainty — that is, they were entirely convinced they had killed him, and mistook that conviction for certainty, when in truth it was mere delusive conjecture. This verse alone demolishes the popular conflation of the intensity of conviction with the validity of certainty: conjecture may reach such psychological firmness that its holder imagines it to be certainty, while the truth is altogether otherwise. True certainty, then, is not the heat of feeling, but the correspondence of knowledge to reality.

### Third: The Three Degrees — From Knowledge to Sight to Truth

If it is settled that true certainty is correspondence with reality rather than intensity of feeling, the next question follows: how is that correspondence attained? The answer lies in a progression stated consecutively in Sūrat al-Takāthur: "No! If only you knew with the knowledge of certainty [ʿilm al-yaqīn]," then "You will surely see it with the eye of certainty [ʿayn al-yaqīn]," completed by the verses of al-Wāqiʿah and al-Ḥāqqah with "the truth of certainty" (ḥaqq al-yaqīn). These are not three synonymous names for one thing, but three entirely different, ascending paths of knowing, none dispensing with the others. Exegetes have illustrated them with the example of fire: to know of its existence from seeing smoke rise is "the knowledge of certainty" — sound rational inference, yet indirect; to see the fire with your own eye is "the eye of certainty" — direct witnessing that dispenses with inference; to enter the fire and be burned by it is "the truth of certainty" — an immersion and lived experience unlike observation from afar.

This same progression holds exactly true for the certainty of faith: the knowledge-of-certainty regarding the Hereafter is what the believer attains by examining proofs and arguments; the eye-of-certainty is a higher degree, when the heart summons the Hereafter to mind in a manner that approaches witnessing, as in the definition of iḥsān: "that you worship God as though you see Him"[^1]; and the truth-of-certainty is the highest degree, when knowledge so mingles with the heart that it becomes conduct rather than information, so that nothing separates what the servant knows from what he does. Whoever is content with the first degree and supposes it the goal of certainty has compressed a long distance into a fleeting pause.

This progression carries a practical effect beyond theory: one who knows the prohibition of usury by proof alone (knowledge-of-certainty) may still fall into it when oversight weakens; one who summons God's displeasure with it in a manner approaching sight (eye-of-certainty) grows ever more wary; and one whose knowledge has so mingled with him that it has become second nature rather than effort (truth-of-certainty) needs no reminder at every transaction. The difference, then, lies not in the correctness of the belief but in the depth of its rootedness — which explains why even the learned scholar sometimes disobeys a ruling he knows well: he may possess the knowledge-of-certainty without having reached its sight or its truth.

### Fourth: Those Described as Being Among the Certain

These three degrees are not mere theoretical abstraction; they are a path a particular human being once walked before the Qurʾan set it down as a rule. The active-participle form "al-mūqinīn" (the certain) occurs tied to one name in particular: Abraham, peace be upon him, when the Qurʾan says: "And thus We showed Abraham the dominion of the heavens and the earth, that he might be of those with certainty [al-mūqinīn]" (al-Anʿām: 75), in the context of his watching the stars, the moon, and the sun set one after another before his heart settled upon the Lord of the worlds. It is striking that certainty did not come to Abraham all at once, but after a journey of observation and repeated refutation — as though the Qurʾan were applying to his own story the very three degrees described above.

And though the verse of Abraham's request to see how the dead are raised does not use the word "certainty" itself, its context is closely related: "My Lord, show me how You give life to the dead... but that my heart may be at rest" (al-Baqarah: 260) — a request to move from the knowledge-of-certainty, which he already believed, to the eye-of-certainty: the request of a prophet who held no doubt, but desired the higher rank.

The other occurrence of the active participle describes a group, not an individual: the leaders of the Children of Israel, of whom God said, "We made from among them leaders guiding by Our command when they were patient and had certainty in Our signs [kānū bi-āyātinā yūqinūn]" (al-Sajdah: 24). The Qurʾan here pairs patience with certainty in a striking way, as though certainty cannot take root except where patience takes root, and as though patience itself is a fruit of certainty rather than a merely separate virtue.

### Fifth: The Witness of the Sunnah — Certainty as an Energy of Steadfastness, Not Dormant Knowledge

And if the Qurʾan paired certainty with patience in the model of the Children of Israel, the Prophetic Sunnah spells out this pairing in practice; it is not content to state certainty as a theoretical concept, but binds it to practical steadfastness under trial. Among its testimonies is his ﷺ counsel to Ibn ʿAbbās, which includes: "and know that what has missed you was not going to strike you, and what has struck you was not going to miss you"[^2] — counsel that addresses not the intellect with an abstract truth, but the heart at the very moment of trial, to steady it. Among them too is the supplication the Prophet ﷺ used to make for his Companions: "O God, grant us of the fear of You that which comes between us and disobedience of You, and of obedience to You that which delivers us to Your Paradise, and of certainty that which lightens for us the calamities of this world"[^3] — an explicit statement that the practical function of certainty is to lighten calamities, not merely to fix a doctrine in place. Certainty, in the guidance of prophethood, is thus an energy spent at the moment of testing, not knowledge stored up for debate.

### Sixth: The Sayings of the Scholars — Certainty as a Station, Not a Waystation

This bond between certainty and conduct, which the Sunnah traced in practice, is what the scholars later elaborated in theory. Ibn al-Qayyim, in *Madārij al-Sālikīn*, devoted a complete station to certainty, counting it the very goal of the wayfarers rather than a passing stage along the path, and holding that the people of certainty are ranked by their excellence in both knowledge and deed together, not in knowledge alone. This accords precisely with the three degrees themselves: whoever is confined to the knowledge-of-certainty has not yet reached what the possessor of the truth-of-certainty has reached, though both share the root of affirmation. And it has been related of some of the verifying scholars that "certainty is to faith as the spirit is to the body," and that when patience and certainty combine, they beget leadership in religion, as the very verse of al-Sajdah declares.

From another angle, Imam al-Ghazālī, in *Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn*, made certainty the root of all devotions of the heart and the body alike, for no outward act is sound except issuing from an inward certainty: the humility of prayer springs from the worshipper's certainty that he stands before One who sees him, and the generosity of charity springs from the giver's certainty that its recompense is guaranteed with God. Here al-Ghazālī meets Ibn al-Qayyim from a different angle: both make certainty a foundation rather than a branch, though they differ in expression — one describes it as a station to be ascended to, the other as a root upon which all else is built.

### Seventh: The Portrait of the Person of Certainty Today

And if certainty was, for these two Imams, a foundation rather than a branch, how much more does contemporary man need to reclaim that very foundation. Contemporary man lives through a crisis of methodical doubt unlike any known before: a philosophical relativism that casts every constant into question, and a flood of information that confuses more than it guides, until many have come to doubt the very possibility of certainty, let alone its attainment. It is precisely in this setting that Qurʾanic certainty recovers its worth — not as a refusal of inquiry or a freezing of the mind, but as a goal attained through the very progression Sūrat al-Takāthur laid out: inference, then witnessing, then immersion. The person of certainty today is not one who holds ready answers to every question, but one who holds a steadiness of heart that withstands questions without collapsing, and who binds his certainty to his patience as the Qurʾan bound the two together in the verse of al-Sajdah — neither awaiting a certainty untested by trial, nor mistaking trial for a refutation of certainty. Furthest of all from true certainty is the one who takes the intensity of his own conviction as proof of its soundness, and the verse of al-Nisāʾ has already exposed this illusion; the measure is not the heat of feeling, but the correspondence of the heart to the truth — even should that correspondence arrive only after a long journey of questioning, rather than a single moment of enthusiasm.

### Conclusion

This progression has returned us to where we began: certainty is not a single state either grasped all at once or not grasped at all, but a journey from inference to witnessing to immersion, which the heart traverses degree by degree — just as Abraham, peace be upon him, traversed it, moving from a star that set, to a moon that set, to a sun that set, until he came to rest upon the One who never sets. And so long as a person remains in this world, the door to moving between the degrees stays open: what knowledge has missed can be recovered by witnessing, and what witnessing has missed can be recovered by immersion, until the final certainty comes, after which there is no further passage.

And God knows best; He is the Grantor of success; there is no lord but Him.
