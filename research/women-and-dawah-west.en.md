### The Terms of the Study

**Woman (al-marʾah):**

- It is stated in *Lisān al-ʿArab*(1): al-marʾ is the human being... A person by his two smallest things: his heart and his tongue. And al-marʾ is the man... maruʾa, yamruʾu. And maraʾah is the feminine of "man," the feminine of the word marʾ, its diminutive being (muraɪyyah)... And imraʾah is the feminine of imruʾ... It also signifies the complete vessel, that is, an indeterminate exalting of her status when one says, "I married a woman (imraʾah)," meaning (a complete one), just as one says, "So-and-so is a man," meaning complete in manhood. And in diminishing the status of a woman one says: al-muraɪyyah... And murʾ (with a ḍammah on the mīm): the man.

The term "woman" may be expressed as follows:

- Woman: an Arabic term denoting the human being whom God Most High created from her partner in life (the man), naming her "female," in parallel with her partner — the man — in the system of legal and life-related rights and duties, differing from him only in what pertains to her nature and physiological functions.

### Daʿwah:

*Al-daʿwah* and *al-duʿāʾ*: the call; and calling to a thing: urging toward seeking it. The call to God: it is summoning to faith in Him and to worshiping Him alone with no partner, to acting in obedience to Him and abandoning disobedience to Him; for God, glorified be He, created creation to worship Him, as He Most High said: "And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me. I do not want from them any provision, nor do I want them to feed Me. Indeed, it is God who is the [continual] Provider, the firm possessor of strength."(2). God has commanded calling to His path, returning the strayed, teaching the ignorant, and reminding the heedless; so He sent down His books and sent His messengers for the sake of calling to Him, and He invited His servants to return to Him through it upon the tongues of His messengers, saying, Most High: "He sends down the angels, with the inspiration of His command, upon whom He wills of His servants, [saying]: Warn that there is no deity except Me; so fear Me."(3).

The intended meaning of daʿwah, in customary usage, is: urging people toward good and guidance, enjoining the right and forbidding the wrong, so that one may attain the happiness of this world and the Hereafter. It is of three kinds:

**The first kind:** the call of the Muhammadan ummah to all nations, inviting them to Islam, and to share in the guidance and the religion of truth that this ummah is upon. This is the duty of this ummah by virtue of its being made the best ummah brought forth for mankind, and by virtue of the description of the believers who were given permission to fight in the words of God Most High: "[They are] those who, if We give them authority in the land, establish prayer and give zakat and enjoin what is right and forbid what is wrong." [al-Ḥajj: 41].

**The second kind:** the call of Muslims to one another toward good, as in His words, glorified be He: "But why does not a company from every group among them go forth..." [al-Tawbah: 122], the verse.

**The third kind:** that which is between individuals among themselves — directing toward good and encouraging it, and forbidding evil and warning against it, as in His words, Mighty and Majestic: "By time. Indeed, mankind is in loss. Except for those who have believed and done righteous deeds and advised each other to truth and advised each other to patience." [al-ʿAṣr: 1–3], and "And who is better in speech than one who invites to God" [Fuṣṣilat: 33], that is, to the religion of God.

**Its conditions are three:**

**The first condition:** that one intends thereby the Face of God, the Exalted and Most High, and that the caller does not seek by it any recompense other than that — neither from the goods of this world, nor from people's praise and commendation of him, nor their drawing near to him.

**The second condition:** that the caller follows therein the way of the Messenger of God ﷺ — not calling according to terms that he himself or some faction around him devises. His daʿwah must be in accordance with the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ.

**The third condition:** that the caller has clear insight (baṣīrah) into his daʿwah — that he has knowledge and understanding of what he calls to, knowing what he himself adheres to of it, and what must be left and avoided. If he is not so, his daʿwah corrupts more than it sets right(1).

If the people of a region or the people of a given country do not undertake daʿwah in full, the sin becomes general, and the duty falls upon all; every person must undertake daʿwah according to his capacity and ability.

So, when callers are few, evils abound, and ignorance prevails, daʿwah becomes an individual obligation (farḍ ʿayn) upon each person according to his capacity. Perhaps this is what the Prophet ﷺ intended in the hadith: "**Whoever among you sees an evil, let him change it with his hand; if he is unable, then with his tongue; and if he is unable, then with his heart — and that is the weakest of faith**"(1). By this it is known that its being an individual obligation and its being a communal obligation (farḍ kifāyah) is a relative matter that varies; daʿwah may be an individual obligation with respect to certain peoples and persons, and a recommended act (sunnah) with respect to other persons and peoples — because within their knowledge and their locale there was found one who undertook the matter and sufficed on their behalf.

**Western society:** By drawing attention to this term I have intended what pertains to our study of the great differences between the two societies, Western and Eastern, especially in matters of daʿwah and the accompanying differences of language, traditions, and cultures.

- As for language: the Islamic legal sciences were originally set down in the Arabic language, with its well-known eloquence and clarity. Hence their grasping is not easy and their direct apprehension is not possible except for specialists and the people of the craft; and that is not easy for the sons and daughters of environments in which Arabic is the second language and not the first.

- As for traditions (al-taqālīd): it is the plural of "tradition" (taqlīd), which is: the totality of what the members of each society have become accustomed to and reckoned among the rules ordering the movement of that society, and which they expect to occur in various situations — that in which they have imitated those before them.

It derives from the verb "qallada, taqlīdan," and it also means that a generation imitates the manners of the generation that preceded it and proceeds upon them — whether in dress, in conduct and behavior, or in the various beliefs and practices that the successors inherit from the predecessors(1).

## The Legal Capacity of Woman in Islam

The natural person is the human being, whether man or woman; for God, Mighty and Majestic, created the human being and singled him out with the capacity for legal accountability, by placing in him the power to understand the address and the ability to choose, so that he becomes fit to comply through acting or refraining.

This capacity is "legal competence" (ahliyyah); that is: the quality by virtue of which the human being becomes capable of having the address of the Lawgiver attach to his actions and statements. It depends fundamentally on humanity, and its basis is soundness of intellect; from a person's being characterized by it follows the establishment of a set of duties upon him, and a set of rights and interests for him, indispensable to his discharge of those duties.

Competence is a condition for all dispositions in which validity and nullity occur; just as religious acts of worship require two kinds of competence — competence for the valid issuance of the act of worship from the person, and competence for the obligation of that act of worship upon him. Penal sanctions likewise, in their various kinds, require for their incurrence — legally and lawfully — the existence in the offender of competence to bear the consequence and the penal responsibility.

Since this study is concerned with the legal competence of woman, arriving at a delineation of the features of woman's competence in Islamic jurisprudence — clarifying the limits of this competence and the extent to which it is independent, deficient, or dependent on another, and tracking how it differs from the competence of man — requires bringing to mind the purpose for which God Most High created the human race upon the earth, namely in His words, Most High: "And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me. I do not want from them any provision, nor do I want them to feed Me. Indeed, it is God who is the [continual] Provider, the firm possessor of strength."

Being charged with worship requires the competence of the one charged in proportion to the nature of this charge. The Prophet's ﷺ treatment of men and women was built upon this legislation, in the way he conferred upon woman the honor of being charged like the man, and in what he demanded of her of rulings, without any difference that would be reckoned a diminishment of her or a stripping of her right. Islam set out to affirm woman's competence in all aspects of religion and worldly life; and these are some of its forms.

## Political Competence

The Prophet's ﷺ pledge-taking (bayʿah) from women just as from men is the best witness to her competence. What draws attention is that the Prophet ﷺ took a pledge from the women of the Anṣār at ʿAqabat Minā before the Hijrah, taking from them the second great pledge upon protection (al-manʿah) — that is, defense — over that from which they protect their own women and children. And he took a pledge from the believers under the tree at al-Ḥudaybiyah that they would not flee from death, in the year six of the Hijrah. As for the pledge of women, its text was mentioned in Sūrat al-Mumtaḥanah, in His words, Most High: "O Prophet, when the believing women come to you pledging to you that they will not associate anything with God, nor steal, nor commit unlawful intercourse, nor kill their children, nor bring forth a slander they have invented between their arms and legs, nor disobey you in what is right — then accept their pledge and ask forgiveness for them of God. Indeed, God is Forgiving and Merciful."(1).

## Her Competence for the Legal Obligations

It is among the matters of consensus — known of the religion by necessity — that upon women is what is upon men of the pillars of Islam, and that she is addressed by all the branches of the Sharia; except that prayer falls away from woman during the time of menstruation and postnatal bleeding absolutely, so she leaves it and does not make it up, owing to its frequency. As for fasting, it falls away from her during her period, and she makes up what she broke of the days of Ramadan, owing to its rarity. As for her Hajj, it is valid in every state, but she does not circumambulate the Sacred House except while in a state of purity.

## Her Competence for Reward and Punishment

God has made woman like man in the matter of reward and punishment, saying: "Whoever does righteousness, whether male or female, while being a believer — We will surely cause him to live a good life, and We will surely give them their reward according to the best of what they used to do."(1). And in some narrations, she granted protection to a man, and her brother ʿAlī, may God ennoble his face, wished to kill him; so she complained of him to the Prophet ﷺ, who granted the man safety and validated her protection.

## Her Competence to Bear Witness and What Follows from It of Concluding Contracts or Executing Legal Rulings

Among the proofs as well is what the Qurʾan stipulated — that woman is exactly like man in the testimony of liʿān (mutual cursing), which the Qurʾan legislated between the spouses when the man accuses his wife and has no witnesses for what he says: "And those who accuse their wives [of adultery] and have no witnesses except themselves — then the witness of one of them shall be four testimonies by God that he is among the truthful. And the fifth that the curse of God be upon him if he should be among the liars. But it will avert the punishment from her if she gives four testimonies by God that he is among the liars. And the fifth that the wrath of God be upon her if he was among the truthful." [al-Nūr: 6–9].

Four testimonies from the man, followed by the invocation of God's curse upon him if he is among the liars, are met and nullified by four testimonies from the woman, followed by the invocation of God's wrath upon her if her husband is among the truthful. This is the justice of Islam in distributing public rights between man and woman, on a plane upon which humanity is realized equally.

- As for their saying that a woman's testimony is half a man's testimony in Islam, the Grand Imam, Shaykh of al-Azhar Shaykh Maḥmūd Shaltūt (1310–1383 AH / 1893–1963 CE), may God have mercy on him, answered this: The words of God, glorified and exalted: "And if there are not two men [available], then a man and two women," are not stated in the context of the testimony by which the judge decides and rules, but rather in the context of guidance toward the means of securing and assuring rights between those dealing with one another at the time of the transaction; for the verse is in His words: "O you who have believed, when you contract a debt for a specified term, write it down. And let a scribe write [it] between you in justice. Let no scribe refuse to write as God has taught him," until His words: "And bring to witness two witnesses from among your men. And if there are not two men, then a man and two women from those whom you accept as witnesses — so that if one of the women errs, then the other can remind her."

So the context is one of securing rights, not of adjudicating by them. The verse guides toward the best kind of securing — that by which the souls of those transacting are reassured concerning their rights.

This does not mean that a woman's testimony, in the testimony of women among whom there is no man, does not establish a right or that the judge does not rule by it; for the utmost that the judiciary requires is "clear evidence" (al-bayyinah).

The erudite scholar Ibn al-Qayyim established that clear evidence in the Sharia is more general than testimony, and that everything by which a right is made clear and manifested is clear evidence by which the judge decides and rules; including this: the judge rules by decisive circumstantial indications, and rules by the testimony of a non-Muslim whenever he trusts it and is reassured by it.

The Qurʾan's reckoning the testimony of a woman in the securing of rights as equal to one man is not due to a weakness of her intellect that would entail a deficiency in her humanity and be an effect of it; rather it is because woman — as Shaykh Muḥammad ʿAbduh said — is not, by nature, occupied with financial transactions and the like exchanges; hence her memory in these is weak, but it is not so in domestic matters, which are her occupation, for in these she is stronger in memory than the man. It is in the nature of human beings generally that their recollection grows stronger for matters that concern them, which they practice and with which they are much occupied.

So the verse came in accordance with what was familiar regarding the affair of woman; and most women are still such — they do not attend the sessions of debt-contracting, nor do they engage in the markets of sale and purchase; and the engagement of some of them in this does not negate this principle that her nature in life requires.

And if the verse guides toward the most complete means of securing rights, and those transacting are in an environment in which women's engagement in sales and attendance at the sessions of debt-contracting predominates, then they have the right to secure their rights through the woman in the same manner as through the man, whenever they are reassured of her recollection and her not forgetting, in the manner of the man's recollection and his not forgetting.

The jurists, moreover, have stipulated that there are cases in which the testimony of a woman alone is accepted — namely the cases whose subjects men are not customarily apprised of, such as childbirth, virginity, the defects of women, and hidden matters.

And that there are cases in which the testimony of a man alone is accepted — namely the cases whose subjects stir the emotion of a woman so that she is not able to bear them. Yet they deemed acceptable her testimony in cases of blood [i.e., wounds and homicide] if it is the only means for establishing the right and for the judge's reassurance by it. And there are cases in which the testimony of both of them together is accepted.

# Chapter Two: Is It Permissible for a Woman to Lecture in Mixed Assemblies of Both Men and Women?

Regarding this question I have observed a vast chasm between, on the one hand, one who reckons woman like man in the scope of movement and mobility — indeed in roaming the earth wherever she wishes, and dressing however she wants, and appearing however she sees fit, without any guardianship over her by anyone, whether a father or a husband — holding that this is among the requirements of civilization and refinement; and, on the other hand, one who says(1) that she:

This is the definition of the righteous woman from the Lord of the Worlds, and whoever is other than her is not righteous; she is the one obedient to God, then to her husband and her parents.

**In answer to this**, we say: We stand between two extremes, each as far from the other as the East is from the West, in terms of the conception of the question and the fundamental view of it.

**First:** the holder of a view of woman as being like man in everything — in physical, intellectual, and psychological strength, in the degree of endurance, patience, and the bearing of burdens upon the self, in self-defense, and in the freedom to move about the earth.

The answer to that: Perhaps what was mentioned is fitting — that God created woman as an independent being with her own self-evident rights in all their recognized aspects; yet He, glorified be He, created in her qualities suited to her being a female, who needs as she is needed, depends as she is depended upon, and is consulted as she consults — without excess that leads to the dissolution of families or the disintegration of societies, without a freedom that reaches the limit of unboundedness, and without rebellion against the original innate nature (fiṭrah) and the nature of life.

I hold that we should not preoccupy ourselves greatly with answering this view, since it contradicts in itself the sound innate nature, and no one deviates to it except one who has known in himself some form of deviance or aberration.

**As for the holders of the latter view** — they are counted among the people of daʿwah and speak in the name of religion — and for the sake of discussion only, I wish to respond that to state unrestrictedly that woman is a thing to be concealed (ʿawrah) and an allurement (fitnah), and that she has no life except through a man, is a statement against her, and that God

did not charge her with jihad or striving, and that her mission in life is confined to being a settled keeper of the home, its mistress. The substance of the answer to this is as follows:

## The Claim That She Is Absolutely an ʿAwrah (to Be Concealed)

What is understood from this word in this context is that woman is a being who ought to vanish from existence and not appear to creation — and this does not accord with the explicit text of the Noble Qurʾan; for God Most High said: "except what [necessarily] appears thereof," indicating that she is present among creation, and that something of her outer garments will be apparent, or what appears of her face and hands, or what is sometimes unavoidable. All of this — at first glance — informs us that she is not an ʿawrah in terms of her presence in society, and that this was not from the guidance of the Prophet ﷺ, nor from the state of those who lived around him ﷺ.

Woman used to appear to society as one of its two halves; the Prophet ﷺ would meet one of them on the road — indeed, a bondwoman (a slave girl) might take his noble hand ﷺ and lead him wherever she wished. By way of evidencing this we cite what was narrated by Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh, may God be pleased with both: My maternal aunt was divorced, and she wished to harvest her palm trees (to cut their fruit), and a man rebuked her for going out, so she came to the Prophet ﷺ, who said: "Yes, harvest your palm trees, for perhaps you may give in charity or do a good deed"(1). So the Prophet ﷺ commanded her in this situation to go forth among the people to do what the men do. You may know that it is concomitant to work in such a profession that the woman raises her hand and some features of her body become apparent... while keeping it covered and unexposed. The Prophet ﷺ did not forbid her from performing her work in society. And it was among the cooperation of the throngs of Companions that each would be to his companion as he is to himself and to his own sister, so that she could find someone to suffice her in this work of hers, someone to cut the fruit for her and bring it back to her, without her enduring that arduous labor herself.

**As for "fitnah":** It is stated in the lexicons of the language that fitnah means: experience, or testing, your being captivated by a thing, misguidance, sin, scandal, torment, and what occurs among people of fighting... The word "fitnah" with its derivatives occurs in the Noble Qurʾan 58 times.

So which of these kinds of fitnah is applied to woman? And as for what the Prophet ﷺ intended when he said: "I have not left behind me any fitnah more harmful to men than women" [and in a narration: "I have not left after me a fitnah more to be feared by my ummah than women"].

Perhaps the most comprehensive of its meanings is that what is intended is testing and trial, as in His words, Most High: "Do the people think that they will be left to say, 'We believe,' and they will not be tried?"(1), and as God Most High said concerning the trial of the believers by the disbelievers: "And We have made some of you a trial for others — will you have patience?"(2), and as He made the rich and the poor a trial for one another: "And thus We have tried some of them through others that they might say, 'Is it these whom God has favored among us?' Is not God most knowing of those who are grateful?"(3).

Accordingly, the fitnah by women means the testing of men; for God placed in the constitution of each of the two sexes — man and woman — an innate inclination toward the other, ever since He created Adam and created for him a mate of his own kind. And the testing is not from every woman the man meets in his life or deals with in some way; rather the fitnah here is what is preceded by woman's susceptibility to committing error and man's impulse toward that, as well as what the work of Satan stirs up of displaying forbidden adornment, or being soft in speech, or the like. He is aided in understanding this by what came from the Prophet ﷺ of associating woman with fitnah in his speech other than his speech about fitnah by women, and likewise the other narrations in which the Prophet ﷺ warned against women who allure God's creation, contriving in speech and deed.

Accordingly, that serious woman, setting forth to God and His Messenger, striving for the truth, speaking with good — she is not the one intended in the making of fitnah. God has had mercy upon the believing women in general in Muslim society, saying: "So righteous women are devoutly obedient, guarding in absence what God would have them guard"(1); thus He described them with the most upright, finest, and most hopeful of qualities for nearness to His servant, glorified be He. And through the other narrations we recognize what kind of woman the Prophet ﷺ reckoned a source of fitnah and seduction.

Perhaps we may fall into a grave error if we judge a woman's going out in striving or work — within its constraints — through the eye of one who views all women as fitnah. Fitnah in Arabic is among the meanings of misguidance, sin, scandal, torment, and what occurs among people of fighting; and not all men are those in whose hearts is disease, so not every woman is a fitnah. I ask whoever reads these words to bring to mind what his mother expended of benefit to society or of reforming it, and what his sister was upon of good conduct or otherwise, and what he heard about his maternal aunt, paternal aunt, or female relative — was she a source of security for society or a source of danger? We may perhaps revere them all as we do our mothers, seeking good from them, hoping for their supplication, and seeking the aid of those of them who have experience in hardships — in the experience of days and the wisdom of nights.

Perhaps what emerges is that woman in olden times, when she went out beside her man into the field to strike the earth with her hoe, brought forth nothing but every benefit to the ummah and humanity, and was held up as a proverb only in strength and the soundness of work, soul, and body.

This set of covenants for woman's setting forth in daʿwah may perhaps come to our minds when we recall the passenger buses where her body is pressed against the body of a man — a woman who cannot flee from him even unto death — or the mixing in the corridors of offices, or the raucous laughter of women who do not possess the modesty that prevents adopting the ways of other than truth, righteousness, and propriety. But the corruption of people does not redound upon the foundation of the legislation so as to alter its realities or change its features.

And [the claim] that her abode is only the home, as a settling place within it, and that woman is not suited except to be under the protection of a man and under his guardianship.

The answer to this: This may perhaps hold true when speaking of marital companionship and the intimacy between two parties, each completing the other, and each deriving from the other what he or she lacks in themselves; for what the woman's heart pours forth of mercy, and what her femininity provides of tenderness and gentleness, is completed in the man by his firmness, valor, strength, protectiveness, solidity, and abundant wisdom — that being in proportion to the primary mission of each of them in life; for he toils on the earth in accordance with what God has commanded, and she gives birth to offspring, which the strongest and mightiest of men cannot do. But she, in any case — as the Prophet ﷺ advised in the incident of the maternal aunt of Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh, that she should go forth to her field to cut the fruit of her palms — is able to lead herself to safety if the man is absent from her life for some reason; and she may excel at raising generations, and at teaching those in her charge if that is entrusted to her. By this I also mean: the woman whom God intended from among the daughters of Eve, and the descendants of Mary, Khadījah, ʿĀʾishah, and their like — not what the idol of history has fashioned.

As for the statement that in His words, Most High: "So righteous women are devoutly obedient, guarding in absence what God would have them guard," there is a definition of the righteous woman, and that whoever is other than her is not righteous — the response is that God Most High divided the women of Muslim society in this verse into two divisions: **the first:** what was mentioned; **and the second:** those who fall into recalcitrance (nushūz); and perhaps this too is a matter pertaining to the question of spouses in the home, and the private relationships between them, and the disagreements that arise in that family over the methodology of managing the home and the family. It is perhaps far-fetched that we should pronounce judgment upon woman in terms of her relationship to all of society.

As for "the devoutly obedient one (al-qānitah)" being the one obedient to God, then to her husband and her parents: there is nothing in that which prevents her from being one who relates good, or calls to a good thing, or speaks with good.

And the statement that God Most High did not mention in the verse of al-Aḥzāb "the striving women," nor "the calling women," nor "the women who enjoin what is right," nor "the women who forbid what is wrong" — likewise He did not mention in the same verse "the striving men," nor "the calling men," nor "the men who enjoin what is right," nor "the men who forbid what is wrong." The description in the noble sūrah came only for the purpose of detailing the competence of both groups for the moral system that safeguards society from the presence of the hypocrites, the obstructers, those in whose hearts is disease, or the alarm-spreaders — of all their kinds.

As for [the claim] that God Most High mentioned "jihad" among the qualities of men in particular in the verse of al-Tawbah, saying: "Indeed, God has purchased from the believers their lives and their properties [in exchange] for that they will have Paradise. They fight in the cause of God, so they kill and are killed," the verse, then said after it: "The repentant, the worshippers..." up to His words: "Those who enjoin what is right and forbid what is wrong" — the address in the verse is shared between men and women, because there is nothing to divert it from its generality, as in His words: "Establish prayer and give zakat"; and because the command to spend in the cause of God is also shared between men and women. And the qualities mentioned in the verse that follows it in the noble sūrah are devotional qualities in which men and women share by agreement.

And when He mentioned the qualities of women in particular — in their claim — saying in the verse of al-Taḥrīm: "Perhaps his Lord, if he divorced you, would replace you with wives better than you — submitting, believing, devoutly obedient, repentant, worshipping, fasting [or traveling for His cause] — previously married and virgins" — and the meaning of "al-sāʾiḥāt" is the fasting women — He mentioned the particular acts of worship, and did not mention among their qualities "trustworthy," or "calling," or "striving," or "seeking reward."

[And they say:] And if combat is not commanded of woman, then likewise the rest of the kinds of jihad if they involve appearing before the public; and accordingly it is not permissible for her — based on the foregoing — to go out from her home under the pretext of calling to God, and that had this been good, Fāṭimah and ʿĀʾishah and the best of the women of the worlds would have preceded her to it. This is the complete view objected to — and the response is in the following pages.

And the answer is that He, glorified be He, when He mentioned the qualities of men in the verse of Āl ʿImrān: "Those who say, 'Our Lord, indeed we have believed, so forgive us our sins and protect us from the punishment of the Fire.' The patient, the truthful, the obedient, those who spend [in the way of God], and those who seek forgiveness before dawn," mentioned only the devotional qualities, and did not mention among them the charge of jihad or striving in the land. This indicates that the mention of some qualities in one place in the Qurʾan does not negate the charge of others. Moreover, we must distinguish between what is obligatory (taklīfī) and what is permissible (mubāḥ); for if God Most High has exempted woman from attending the congregational and Friday prayers, that is no evidence of prohibition against their attending the prayers or witnessing the congregations. Testifying to this is what was reported from Ibn ʿUmar, who said: A wife of ʿUmar used to attend the dawn and night prayer in congregation in the mosque, and she was asked: Why do you go out when you know that ʿUmar dislikes that and is jealous? She said: And what prevents him from forbidding me? He said: He is prevented by the saying of the Messenger of God ﷺ: "Do not prevent the bondwomen of God from the mosques of God"(1).

## And Between the Two Extremes There Is a Middle Course

That woman be a part of society, that she appreciate what is incumbent upon her in safeguarding this society through chastity and purity, that she earn the trust of those around her through her effort and her effective performance, and that she receive from them good intention, good treatment, and purity of hearts. For as we have mentioned: not every woman is a temptress, nor is every man a debauchee — otherwise we would be speaking of throngs other than Muslim society.

- It was reported from Jābir ibn ʿAbd Allāh, who said: "While we were seated with the Messenger of God ﷺ, a woman came to him and said: O Messenger of God, I am the delegate of the women to you: God, Mighty and Majestic, is the Lord of men and the Lord of women, and Adam is the father of men and the father of women, and Eve is the mother of men and the mother of women; and God, Mighty and Majestic, sent His Prophet to men and women. So the men, when they go out in the cause of God and are killed, are alive with their Lord, provided for; and when they go out, they have of reward what they have...

In the narration of Ibn ʿAbbās, may God be pleased with both, he said: "A woman came to the Prophet ﷺ and said: O Messenger of God, I am the delegate of the women to you: God has prescribed jihad upon men, so that if they are successful they are rewarded, and if they are killed they are with their Lord, provided for; and we, the company of women, attend to them — so what is there for us of that? The Prophet ﷺ said: Convey to whomever of the women you meet that a wife's obedience to her husband and her acknowledgment of his right equals that, but few of you do it."

Perhaps a man rose up against his wife [claiming] that she is restricted in step and movement and that she is incapable, so she wished to take as evidence against him what the Messenger of God ﷺ would use to mend her heart — and the Prophet ﷺ treated her well when she took as evidence her statement in the other narration:

Indeed, God, blessed and exalted, sent you to men and women, so we believed in you and in your God who sent you; and indeed God has preferred you over us — the company of men — with the congregation, the Friday prayer, visiting the sick, following funerals, and, more excellent than that, jihad in the cause of God. And when one of you goes out as a fighter, a pilgrim, or a performer of ʿumrah, we guard your wealth, weave your garments, and raise your children for you; and we, the company of women, are confined and restricted, the keepers of your homes — so shall we not share with you in this reward?" A woman's speaking of the matters particular to her and to her relationship with her husband was not forbidden to be presented before the Messenger ﷺ and his noble Companions in the public circle, or during the Prophet's ﷺ speech to his Companions — a woman speaking with her dignity, her complete esteem for the one calling her, her faith, the goodness of her expression, and the chastity of her phrasing in speech.

This indicates the permissibility of a woman lecturing in assemblies mixed between men and women together, with the legal constraints that the discussion has comprised.

## Is It Permissible for Her to Participate in a Television Interview Watched by Millions via Satellite, While She Is Among Those Who Do Not Hold the Covering of the Face to Be Obligatory?

After reviewing a number of fatwas published by our shaykhs on the Internet(1), I observed that there is a semblance of objection or a view holding the impermissibility of a woman appearing on media screens.

The fatwas relied, for the most part, on the following:

**First:** The male is not like the female, and woman differs from man in form, external organs, and general features, as well as in what befalls her of menstruation and the like; this requires that woman undertake a role suited to her nature and her bodily function.

**Second:** The most important thing that woman undertakes and attends to is her home; for the function of motherhood, the family, and the home is among the most exalted and important of functions, and one ought never to be lax in this or diminish its standing.

**Third:** Woman may direct her effort toward improving her appearance and image for the viewers.

**Fourth:** It may happen that seclusion (khalwah) occurs with men unrelated to her during recording, preparation, and the like.

**Fifth:** Many media personnel bring a woman into this field only to attract the gaze of those who do not restrain their sight; for it is well known that woman adds nothing new to the bulletin or the program.

And the answer to them — as it appears, and we ask God for soundness in religion and safety in speech — is:

1. "The male is not like the female" is a slogan used by whoever cites it wherever he wishes, and we must distinguish between her being like the man as a human being and in right and duty, and her not being like the man in what the context of the verses concerned — what

> Note (1): Fatwas published on websites (IslamWeb and the al-Muslim forum).

was the state of the Children of Israel of not recognizing woman as a being capable of discharging the duty as the man does. And that is not, in itself, an impediment to the works that may be assigned to her — those she is capable of, within her ethical constraints. These new media — with the good in them as mentioned — are in greater need of righteous personnel, both men and women, given the differences in natures, cultures, and educational methods that each of the man and the woman possesses.

2. As for her directing her effort toward improving her image for the viewers: that is fundamentally built upon ill suspicion; and if a woman is to be prevented from going out adorned in her finery, then how about her going out before the masses via the screen?! Accordingly, we permit the matter here for one who knows what is for her and what is upon her, in terms of the constraints of dress and adornment and not being soft in speech. And we may find her expression where she appears as the example that draws in those who listen to her or watch her.

3. As for woman not adding anything to the media space: this conception is sound if we restrict it to a news bulletin or a political interview; but if there is beneficial knowledge in a specialty in which she has excelled, or a dialogue about a matter pertaining to the daughters of her sex, or something with which she has a connection and in which she is more influential — then the matter differs.

4. Seclusion with men during recording: those who have lived in these settings know that the recording of episodes or programs is not free of the presence of camera operators, those in charge of lighting, the director, the assistants, and others. The matter more deserving of investigation in this question is if she is alone in the midst of all these; and then the matter depends on the presence of safe company or the presence of a maḥram. This is

the same problem that arises for woman whenever she is at any work with men; and it is a matter to be referred back to in the ruling on whether woman's work is permissible or not, or the legal constraints on woman's work.

In this particular reality, we ought not to forget that she has become a professor at a university whom her students see — whether we like it or not — and that she has become a physician whom her patients enter upon — also, whether we like it or not — and that by closing such doors we fear that other doors may be opened against us that we cannot close. If we have any work and diligence, it is to strive for reform as much as we are able, against what may seep into the soul and weaken it thereby — such as the love of fame, or being deluded by stardom, or chasing after wealth — all of which are temptations that befall men and women alike.

## Is It Permissible for Her to Undertake the Role of Program Host in These Public Sessions, Despite the Existence of Young Men Who Do This Well?

This question specifically requires that I search for the preventer or the permitter; for the basic principle — as we mentioned in Islam's description of woman — is that she is that being independent in her self and identity, to whom the religion has guaranteed rights and whom it has charged with duties. And undertaking tasks particular to her may sometimes accord with her academic study or her own pastime; so the locus of the discussion is not whether or not the opportunity is afforded to her, but rather its locus is:

What are the constraints with which a woman or a girl must guard herself when undertaking this work of hers? The greatest of our reasons for prohibition is the media field itself; for its reputation among people was, for a long time, not a good one, and it cast confusion upon those who did good, so we reckoned the ruling that the bad spreads and the good is particular. Perhaps this view needs to change, and that will not be except by renewing the blood in this field.

We lived for a time when we knew in Egypt only Channel One and Channel Two, both governmental, broadcasting to the people what the people of politics wanted; so the general ruling on watching television was dislike, except for what was lawful in it, such as the abundance of news, and among what marred it was the sight of "the clothed yet naked," as came in the hadith. So if the years come and those working in the field of daʿwah ascend — for the blood must be renewed and the mental image among the public altered through the visual image via the satellites — we would see the eminent figures of the ummah pouring upon us from what God has poured upon them of good and knowledge.

Likewise the present reality needs to alter the general scene of woman, whom people know via the screen only as a naked body, or as a means of promoting a newly devised fashion of dress, or some other commodity.

So there is no objection to our altering the media scene by the very same means, with reservation as to the constraints. And what prevents there being an academic department for media in our Islamic universities throughout the world, from which graduate women callers who know the right of God and the right of humanity, and who excel at promoting virtue at a time when its features have become lost? And we ask God for soundness.

Among the most that we can find fault with when watching a program (of any kind) presented by a woman is: that she flirts by a gesture of her eye with her guest — in terms of his appearance — or that this appears as a belittling of his standing before the program's viewers by the method of influence through gesture; or that she looks at the camera plainly, with the cameraman's artistry in displaying her looks of admiration, displeasure, anger, or delight, and so forth.

So if there is found the woman who lowers her gaze and does not raise it except to the extent the situation permits — and how excellent if she hosts in her program well-protected women of knowledge and jurisprudence — and if there is found the cameraman who knows the limit of God in displaying what appears of a woman's adornment, and the clothing is not striking in itself except toward a kind of cleanliness and non-vulgarity — then there is no objection to the woman or the sister presenting her program, in accordance with the criteria we have mentioned. And God Most High knows best.

The flaw evident now is in the deficiencies of the good presentation of daʿwah in word and deed; so if we do not find among the people of knowledge one who permits a woman now to present a dialogue or radio program, perhaps the reason until now is the absence of the model to be emulated. Hence the matter is connected to the contrivances of creation, and not to the foundations of the Sharia.

## Is It Permissible, If Her Husband Prevents Her from Participating in Daʿwah Activities — Despite Her Not Falling Short in Her Marital Duties — for Her to Reckon That as Harm to Her? And Is She Sinful If She Requests khulʿ or Separation for Harm for This Reason?

If the ruling on a thing is a derivative of its conception, then I first wish to posit the reasons that might lead a man to prevent his wife from working in daʿwah, and the timing of this prevention.

If that is at the outset of agreeing on the marriage, and he does not want her from the beginning to work in this field, then acquiescing to their terms and what the contract will be built upon is what will govern her — whether by responding to his desire or rejecting it; and she may refuse to marry him if she sees that she will not fulfill what the contract's agreement will be. The text is explicit: "O you who have believed, fulfill [your] contracts."(1).

And if his preventing her is after her reputation has spread and her knowledge has become widespread, then the reason is examined: is it the motive of fear for her, or a pulling of jealousy and the like? Such matters are estimated according to their measure, and only the people of knowledge — those acquainted with the state of this husband and this woman — issue a fatwa on them.

I say this because the general ruling on this question leads to severe contention between spouses, and what follows from it of the demolition of homes and the displacement of offspring, who are the primary objective of marriage. What complicates the matter further is that the eminent figures and jurists of the ummah did not permit a woman to go out of her husband's house except with his permission, even to visit her father or mother; including the following:

Shaykh al-Islam Ibn Taymiyyah, may God have mercy on him, said in the *Fatāwā*: "When a woman marries, her husband has more right over her than her father, and her obedience to her husband is more obligatory."

> Note (1): From verse 1 of Sūrat al-Māʾidah.

And he also said: "So she may not go out of his home except with his permission, whether her father or her mother or someone other than her father commanded her — by the agreement of the imams."

And Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī said in *al-Fatāwā al-Fiqhiyyah al-Kubrā* — after mentioning the necessary cases in which it is permissible for a woman to go out without her husband's permission —: "Not to visit a sick person even if he be her father, nor for his death and attending his funeral."

And Ibn Qudāmah said in *al-Mughnī*: "The husband may prevent her from going out of his home, toward that which she can do without, whether she wishes to visit her parents, tend to them in illness, or attend the funeral of one of them." Aḥmad said concerning a woman who has a husband and an ill mother: "Her obedience to her husband is more obligatory upon her than [obedience to] her mother, unless he permits her."

I am not one to comment upon the statements of the people of knowledge in this question, out of respect for their words and their standing. I understand from this that she must obey her husband in leaving the duty that is dutifulness to parents — so what then of voluntary daʿwah?

We then ask: What if daʿwah is at the level of an obligation, such that there is no one to take her place? God Most High said: "And it is not for the believers to go forth all together. So why does not a company from every group among them go forth to obtain understanding in religion and warn their people when they return to them, that they might be cautious." So does the ruling of obeying him and not disobeying him in that remain, when we have learned that "that without which the obligation is not fulfilled is itself an obligation"?

And what if his preventing her is for a need in himself, at the time when she is keen to keep both her daʿwah and her home at one time?!

These are but some questions that require of us a kind of deliberateness in such a ruling, and that lead us to refer it back to the individual cases, each of which is judged on its own. And God is the One whose help is sought.

As for her right to request khulʿ from him on this account or not, the question is like the one preceding it — it depends on the degree of difference in viewpoints between the two parties; for no sensible person would say that woman in this case is like a machine that works or stops at the press of a button or the signal of a finger. The matter requires examination of the true motives behind his preventing her, and likewise behind her insistence on proceeding; then the jurist gives preponderance to one of the two scales based on the evidence before him.

As for whether she is sinful if she requests khulʿ from him or not, the answer to it is:

**First:** Knowledge of hearts is known to God alone, and apprehending them is difficult; so refraining from [judging] them is preferable to ruling on them, since they are built upon the unseen.

**Second:** Her condition is examined by the following constraints: the degree of her fulfillment of her husband's right and her home's right, and whether her fulfillment of the home's right is a mere claim or a reality in the husband's acknowledgment and in fact; whether she is qualified for daʿwah work; and the degree of society's or the daʿwah work's need for her. If she reaches some measure of that, and has fulfilled her husband's right and what the Sharia has charged her with toward him, and she is qualified for the work and not a pretender in it, and giving through her is of hoped-for fruit and abundant yield, and the need for her is pressing — as is the case in the lands whose reality we now live — then there is no sin upon her; rather the sin is upon him, for he stands between her and the benefit that would redound to society through her, and what would protect society from the sins of temptations to the extent she is able to reach that.

**Third:** It is important that we note the importance of her advising her husband — if he is sound of heart, keen on the religion — of the importance of what she does, and that they will both attain before God Most High the reward of what He has given her of knowledge and ability.

But among the counsels is: that a woman caller ought not to do a work with which she senses her husband's anger — which may be followed, if the husband is ignorant, by the anger of God Most High — or that she should set right the homes of people and their affairs at the time when she corrupts her own home and family.

## Is It Permissible for Her to Participate in Public Daʿwah Gatherings in the Street or at Conferences and Symposiums? And Is It Permissible for Her to Admonish Men in a Mosque from Behind a Screen or Directly? For Example, to Give a Reflection (khāṭirah) in the Month of Ramadan? Or to Answer Juristic Questions for Both Men and Women?

It has already been clarified that the woman whom Islam raised, whose rights it preserved, whose standing it elevated, and whom it honored, has been made an example to be emulated; that she must undertake her duty as a mother, a rearer of generations, and a teacher of good, in the best manner, and that she should contribute through her knowledge and effort toward raising the banner of Islam and enlightening the daughters of her sex with what redounds to them in fruitful benefit.

Among what is upon her is: calling to God and enjoining the right and forbidding the wrong — with the legal etiquette pertaining to her as a woman; and upon her is organizing her time among her duties, and arranging her priorities between elevating herself in faith and adorning herself with acts of worship, caring for her husband and her home, raising her children, and connecting with them under the circumstances of the age — so as to achieve a balance between her message in her home and what surrounds her family in the outer society.

If we trace the Islamic history of woman, we find that the Muslim woman struck the finest example and model for the daughters of her sex in her knowledge, her cultivation, and her keenness to receive knowledge from its authentic sources and to act upon it. ʿĀʾishah, may God be pleased with her, struck for us the finest example of the Muslim woman's devotion to learning; for she, may God be pleased with her, was distinguished by her copious, vast knowledge in various fields of the sciences: such as hadith, medicine, poetry, jurisprudence, and the inheritance shares. Imam al-Zuhrī said of her: "If the knowledge of ʿĀʾishah were gathered alongside the knowledge of all the Mothers of the Believers and the knowledge of all women, the knowledge of ʿĀʾishah would be the best of it."

Hishām ibn ʿUrwah said: "I have not seen anyone more knowledgeable in jurisprudence, medicine, or poetry than ʿĀʾishah." She, may God be pleased with her, was intense in verification and inquiry; for al-Mizzī mentioned that she would not hear anything she did not know without reviewing it until she knew it. She is also counted among the prolific narrators of hadith, and after the death of the Messenger ﷺ she was a magnificent model of the household of prophecy, and a beacon of knowledge...

Among the proofs is: a woman's speaking in the mosque, present among men and women — what was already referred to of the hadith of Asmāʾ bint Yazīd, nicknamed "the delegate of the women."

The matter — as before — requires examination of the degree of need for her, the knowledge she possesses, and society's acceptance of her appearing in mosques, public circles, symposiums, or public daʿwah gatherings; and that there be no urging or aspiration for woman to be like man in the sermons of the mosques, and what may result from it of the demand that she lead the prayer so that the aspects of equality might be completed...

# Recommendations

1. Woman's experience with the media in the past was not a good experience at all levels, but it was in the field of what is called art and acting; and we perhaps need to counter this flaw with its like, with a difference in the aim and the direction of the means toward woman's appearance in the daʿwah space, so that more good models to be emulated in real life and in religious commitment may be opened for our sons and daughters. We must reassess the faith environments that include women, and the diseased societies whose harm to our daughters we fear, even if they are in the depths of their homes.

2. The constraints on woman's participation in the media are the constraints of responsibility generally, for her and for the man; namely that the participation not be contrary to the Sharia in terms of the ideas presented and the views called to. There are constraints particular to woman that differ according to the type of media outlet in which she participates; including that she must not display her allurements — whether in visual media or the press — just as it is forbidden for her to be soft in speech and to soften her words in a way that attracts men when she participates in audio media.

3. There is no one able to find for woman how she may be an effective member of society except herself; for the Muslim woman who carries sound thought and awareness will surely find the appropriate field through which she serves her society in a way that does not conflict with her religion, and is an effective member in it. The cases are vast, scarcely to be enumerated, but woman must choose what suits her in terms of time, inclination, and desire, and what she sees as bringing about positive results, without her work redounding with negatives upon herself and her family.

4. There must be a reconsideration of the structuring of the channels and programs that address the issues of the ummah, and of woman's presence in them in a manner more abundant for her entity and standing, and more protective of her from the confrontations she may face; such as that women's programs employ, in their production and administrative structure, a group of specialized women who are attentive to what ought and ought not to be. By this we find the middle solution in the crisis in which opinions are divided over this question.
