*By Dr. Muhammad Attia Metwally, Professor of Daʿwah and Islamic Culture, American Imams Academy.*

## Introduction: An Offer Without Peer, and a Refusal Without Peer

When the Emigrants (the Muhājirūn) arrived in Medina carrying nothing of their worldly goods, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) established brotherhood between them and the Helpers (the Anṣār), so as to make brotherhood in religion a recompense for the wealth, family, and home they had lost. Among those whom this blessed pact of brotherhood joined together were two men whose first meeting became a proverb told to this very day: Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ the Anṣārī, and ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAwf al-Zuhrī (may Allah be pleased with them both).

Al-Bukhārī related in his Ṣaḥīḥ, on the authority of ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAwf, who said: When we came to Medina, the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) established brotherhood between me and Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ. Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ said: "I am the wealthiest of the Anṣār, so I will divide my wealth in half for you; and look at whichever of my two wives you like, and I will divorce her for you, and once her waiting period is complete you may marry her." ʿAbd al-Raḥmān said to him: "I have no need of that. May Allah bless you in your family and your wealth. Is there a marketplace with trade in it?" He said: "The marketplace of Qaynuqāʿ." So ʿAbd al-Raḥmān went there early and brought back some dried curd and clarified butter, then he kept going early, and it was not long before he came with the traces of yellow perfume upon him. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said to him: "Have you married?" He said: "Yes." He said: "And what did you give as dowry?" He said: "The weight of a date-stone of gold." So he (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "Hold a wedding feast, even if only with a single sheep."

In this brief scene two virtues stand face to face, each having reached its utmost: a giving that knows no limit, whose owner offers half of his wealth though he is the wealthiest of the Anṣār; and a chastity that knows no craving, whose owner returns all of that with a comprehensive word and a sincere supplication, and then asks about the marketplace. The question upon which this article rests is: what did time do to the wealth of the two men? And what fruit did the giving of Saʿd and the chastity of ʿAbd al-Raḥmān bear when we trace the two fortunes through the registers of the Sunna, the Prophetic biography, and history?

## First: Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ — Giving at Its Summit

Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ ibn ʿAmr was an Anṣārī of the Khazraj, of the clan of al-Ḥārith ibn al-Khazraj, one of the twelve chieftains (naqībs) on the Night of ʿAqaba, and one of the few who could write in the Age of Ignorance. He witnessed Badr and was martyred at Uḥud in the third year after the Hijra.

His offer to his emigrant brother was no rhetorical courtesy, but a division ready to be carried out at once: "I will divide my wealth in half for you." He is the one who said of himself — as a report, not a boast — "I am the wealthiest of the Anṣār." Half his wealth, then, was an immense real fortune offered to a man to whom, only days before, he was bound by neither kinship nor acquaintance. Indeed, he went beyond wealth to what is dearer than it when he offered to give up one of his two wives for him — after her divorce and the completion of her waiting period, as the scholars have clarified that the offer pertained only to what would come after the lawful separation. This is a degree of self-sacrifice for which human societies have known no equal, so much so that the biographers said: by this character the Anṣār were the most faithful of people to the bond of brotherhood.

Then Saʿd continued upon the path of giving to its very end. For on the Day of Uḥud, when the people were routed, the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "Who will go and see for me what has become of Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ?" A man of the Anṣār found him among the slain, with a last breath still in him, and upon him were some seventy-odd strikes of the sword, thrusts of the spear, and shots of the arrow. He said: "Tell my people, the Anṣār: you have no excuse before Allah if your Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) is reached while a single eye among you yet blinks." Then his soul departed. So the man who gave half his wealth on the first day of the brotherhood gave all his blood on the last day of his life, and he died before the world was opened up to the Muslims, leaving behind none of the vast fortune that his emigrant brother left. Where, then, is the fruit of his giving?

## Second: The Fruit of Giving — Saʿd's Wealth Becomes an Eternal Ruling

Here falls the great subtlety that the tracing of the wealth in the books of the Sunna uncovers. For after Saʿd's martyrdom, his wife came to the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) with his two daughters and said: "O Messenger of Allah, these are the two daughters of Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ; their father was killed alongside you at Uḥud as a martyr, and their uncle took their wealth and left them nothing, and they will not be married unless they have wealth." He (peace and blessings be upon him) said: "Allah will decree concerning that." Then the verse of inheritance was revealed: ﴿Allah instructs you concerning your children﴾ [al-Nisāʾ: 11]. So the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) sent for their uncle and said: "Give Saʿd's two daughters two-thirds, give their mother one-eighth, and what remains is yours." Al-Tirmidhī said: a ḥasan ṣaḥīḥ ḥadīth; and the scholars have noted that this was the first inheritance divided in Islam according to the requirement of the verse of inheritances.

Reflect, then: the man who spread out half his wealth, willingly and by choice, for his brother in Allah, was not left by Allah after his death to the division of the Age of Ignorance that deprived daughters; rather, Allah sent down concerning his wealth — that very wealth — a Qurʾan to be recited until the Day of Resurrection, by which the wealth of daughters, orphans, and widows throughout the entire nation of Muhammad (peace and blessings be upon him) is safeguarded until the Hour arises. Saʿd's wealth was the very case by which Allah opened the door of the science of inheritance shares (farāʾiḍ), so that every male and female heir in this nation now takes his due by the blessing of a case whose title is: the estate of Saʿd ibn al-Rabīʿ.

And there is a still finer subtlety: that ʿAbd al-Raḥmān's supplication on the day of the brotherhood — "May Allah bless you in your family and your wealth" — was fulfilled in Saʿd's family and wealth to the fullest fulfillment; for his family was safeguarded by revelation descending from above the seven heavens, and his wealth was immortalized in the Book of Allah and the Sunna of His Messenger (peace and blessings be upon him). Giving diminished Saʿd's wealth in nothing; rather it raised it from wealth that is inherited to a legislation that bequeaths. This is the fruit of giving in the case of Saʿd: a transitive, eternal blessing not to be reckoned in dinars.

## Third: ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAwf — Chastity at Its Summit

ʿAbd al-Raḥmān's refusal was neither the disdain of arrogance nor a belittling of his brother's offer, but the chastity of a soul, trust in Allah's provision, and reverence for earning. He met the noblest of offers with the noblest of replies: a supplication for blessing, and a question about the marketplace. Then he translated chastity into action: he went early to the marketplace of Banū Qaynuqāʿ, trading in clarified butter and dried curd, and he kept going early until he married from his own earnings for the weight of a date-stone of gold. The Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) blessed him and commanded him to hold a feast.

Then Allah opened for him the doors of provision to such a degree that he described it himself, saying: "I saw myself such that, were I to lift a stone, I would hope to find beneath it gold or silver." His trade grew so great that a caravan of seven hundred mounts came to him bearing wheat, flour, and food, and when it entered Medina, the city trembled for it. So the chastity that returned the half-wealth of a single man — to it Allah drove, after some years, caravans that made Medina shake.

## Fourth: Tracing ʿAbd al-Raḥmān's Fortune — The Figures of Charity and Estate

The books of biographical layers (Ṭabaqāt) and history have preserved for us the figures of this fortune, both in giving and in estate. Among his charities during his lifetime: he gave half his wealth in charity in the time of the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) — four thousand — then he gave forty thousand in charity, then forty thousand dinars; he provided five hundred horses in the path of Allah, and five hundred mounts; he sold a piece of land of his for forty thousand dinars and distributed it among the poor of Banū Zuhra, the needy of the Emigrants, and the Mothers of the Believers, so much so that ʿĀʾisha (may Allah be pleased with her), when word of her share reached her, said: "May Allah give the son of ʿAwf to drink from Salsabīl in Paradise." At his death he bequeathed four hundred dinars to every one who remained of the People of Badr — and they were a hundred, and they took it, until even ʿUthmān ibn ʿAffān took it, despite the abundance of his own wealth, and said: "ʿAbd al-Raḥmān's wealth is lawful and pure." After all of that he bequeathed fifty thousand dinars in the path of Allah.

Then came the estate; he passed away (may Allah be pleased with him) in the year thirty-two after the Hijra. Ibn Saʿd and al-Dhahabī mentioned that he left gold that was chopped with axes until the hands of the men were blistered by it; he left a thousand camels, a hundred horses, and three thousand sheep grazing at al-Baqīʿ; and his wives were four, and their one-eighth was divided so that each received eighty thousand — and Tumāḍir bint al-Aṣbagh, who had been divorced during his final illness, was settled with regarding a quarter of the eighth for eighty thousand. The ḥāfiẓ Ibn Ḥajar estimated the total of the estate at three million two hundred thousand. So if the share of a single wife from the eighth of the estate was eighty thousand, the observer grasps what a sea of wealth belonged to the man who one day said: "Direct me to the marketplace."

And with this immense wealth, his soul remained upon its first chastity — fearful, not seduced. Al-Bukhārī related that ʿAbd al-Raḥmān ibn ʿAwf was brought food while he was fasting, and he said: "Muṣʿab ibn ʿUmayr was killed, and he was better than I; he was shrouded in a cloak such that if his head were covered his feet showed, and if his feet were covered his head showed. And Ḥamza was killed, and he was better than I. Then the world was spread out for us as it was spread out — and we have feared that our good deeds may have been hastened for us." Then he began to weep until he left the food. This is the precise scale: a hand that manages millions, and a heart that weeps for fear that its good things may have been hastened for it. As for what has become well-known upon the tongues — the ḥadīth of his entering Paradise crawling because of the greatness of his wealth — it is a weak ḥadīth that is not established. What is firmly established and authentic is that he was one of the ten given glad tidings of Paradise, and one of the six people of the Consultation (Shūrā) with whom the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings be upon him) passed away while pleased.

## Fifth: The Comparison — What Did Giving Bear, and What Did Chastity Bear?

If we set the two life-stories side by side, fruits appear that a single root gathers together: that what is for Allah is neither lost nor diminished.

As for Saʿd: he gave half his wealth and it was returned to him with his brother's blessed supplication; then he gave his blood and was raised a martyr; then Allah took charge of his wealth after him and sent down concerning it the verse of inheritances. So the fruit of his giving became not confined to his children, but extended into every house of the houses of the Muslims in which an estate is divided. He gave half his wealth, and his giving bore the safeguarding of the wealth of the whole nation. Nor did his self-sacrifice diminish his daughters' portion in anything; for the revelation that was occasioned by their wealth guaranteed them two-thirds by a decisive text.

As for ʿAbd al-Raḥmān: he abstained from his brother's wealth, and Allah made flow through his hands wealth that made his charities alone — of caravans, horses, and dinars — many times over the half he had abstained from; confirming the words of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him): "Whoever seeks chastity, Allah will keep him chaste; and whoever seeks sufficiency, Allah will make him sufficient." His chastity was not deprivation, but the key to wealth; for the one who refuses to take from people, Allah opens for him to give to people. And the roles reversed at the end of the scene in a subtle reversal: the emigrant to whom half a fortune had been offered and who refused it became himself the one spending upon the widows of the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him), the People of Badr, and the poor of Medina, so much so that it was said: the people of Medina are all partners of Ibn ʿAwf in his wealth — a third he lends, a third whose debts he settles, and a third with which he maintains kinship ties.

Moreover, in the joining of the two models there is a lesson in the jurisprudence of Prophetic upbringing: for the Prophet (peace and blessings be upon him) neither rebuked Saʿd for his generosity nor commanded ʿAbd al-Raḥmān to accept; rather he left the two virtues to complete each other, because the nation needs the giver who holds nothing back, just as it needs the chaste one who craves nothing. Had ʿAbd al-Raḥmān accepted, there would have been in the story a single generous man; but when he refused, there came to be in it two generous men, and there remained for the generations a twofold image: an upper hand that gives, and a hand that refuses to be a lower one while it is capable of earning.

## Conclusion

We have traced two fortunes: a fortune half of which was given in a single sitting, then sealed by its owner with his own blood, so that it bore a decisive verse and a science of inheritance shares by which estates are divided until the Day of Resurrection; and a fortune that began with the return of a gift and a question about the marketplace, so that it bore caravans that made Medina shake, charities that gave amply to the Mothers of the Believers and the People of Badr, and an estate over whose counting the historians were bewildered — while its owner wept over his food for fear that his good things had been hastened for him. So Saʿd lost nothing by giving, and ʿAbd al-Raḥmān was deprived of nothing by chastity; rather blessing was the ally of both, because its axis is not the withholding of wealth or its bestowal, but the heart that deals with Allah in both states. And Allah the Exalted has spoken the truth: ﴿And whatever you spend, He will replace it, and He is the best of providers﴾ [Sabaʾ: 39].
