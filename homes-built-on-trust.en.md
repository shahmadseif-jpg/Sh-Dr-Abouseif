We usually read the Hijra as an epic of blood and sacrifice: wealth abandoned, homes forsaken, necks bared to the sword for the sake of faith. That reading is entirely true. Yet in the record of the Hijra there is a single household whose story, once you reflect on it, opens a door no less profound: that the Hijra was also a school of steadfast marriage, and a sign among the signs of God's concealment (sitr) — His sheltering of His servant — by which He musters from the soldiers of the heavens and the earth whoever will guard a believer's honor and dignity. It is the household of Abū Salama, ʿAbd Allāh ibn ʿAbd al-Asad, and his wife Umm Salama, Hind bint Abī Umayya al-Makhzūmiyya, may God be pleased with them both.

In this household two things came together that rarely meet in a single story: a sacrifice that breaks the heart, and a marital trust that does not waver even when the days tear the family apart and scatter it across the land. Through these two threads the larger meaning shines through: that when a servant is true with God, God takes charge of sheltering him — even if He brings that shelter at the hand of an idolater who had not yet entered into faith.


## A Home Before It Was a Journey

Abū Salama was no passing figure among the Companions of God's Messenger ﷺ. He was the son of the Prophet's paternal aunt, Barra bint ʿAbd al-Muṭṭalib, and his foster-brother — both nursed by Thuwayba, the freedwoman of Abū Lahab. He was among the very first to embrace Islam, among the first to emigrate to the land of Abyssinia, and then — as the reports say — at the head of those who emigrated to Medina, so much so that he was counted the first emigrant to it, or among the first.

Umm Salama, too, came from a house of nobility in Quraysh. Her father was Abū Umayya ibn al-Mughīra al-Makhzūmī, one of the most generous of the Arabs, nicknamed "the Traveler's Provision" (Zād al-Rākib) because he would never let a companion on a journey carry his own provisions — he would suffice him. She was a woman raised in honor and high lineage, who then preferred above all of it her religion and her husband.

When we look into the account of their emigration, we do not see two spouses who chanced upon one another on a road; we see a household founded on mutual trust: a husband who knows his wife's worth, and a wife who knows her husband's standing in the religion. This trust is the key to the entire story — and it is what would shortly be tested with a trial that only a home built on a firm foundation could endure.

Among the subtle workings of providence is that these two spouses were joined by lineage in a single tribe, the Banū Makhzūm, the most fortified and wealthy of the houses of Quraysh. Their departure from Mecca, then, was no flight from weakness in search of honor; it was a stripping-away of an honor they already possessed for the sake of a more lasting one. They left behind noble lineage, leadership, and a protecting clan, and chose an exile with no clan and no standing, possessing along its road nothing but faith and their trust in one another. This alone reveals the mettle of the household before the journey even began.


## When a Family Is Torn Apart Before the Husband's Eyes

Abū Salama resolved upon emigration. He set his wife and son upon a camel and set out with them toward Medina. But when the men of the Banū al-Mughīra — Umm Salama's people — saw him, they barred his way, saying: "As for yourself, you have overcome us. But what of this companion of ours? On what grounds should we let you travel the land with her?" They tore the camel's halter from his hand and took her from him.

At this the Banū ʿAbd al-Asad — Abū Salama's clan — grew angry and said: "We will not leave our son with her, since you have wrenched her from our man." They pulled the little boy, Salama, back and forth between them until they dislocated his arm, and they carried him off. So Abū Salama went on to Medina alone. In a single hour three separations fell upon this household: a husband in one land, a child in another's keeping, and a mother torn in heart between them.

Umm Salama said of those days: "I would go out every morning and sit in al-Abṭaḥ, and I would keep weeping until evening — for a year, or close to it." A full year of weeping in the open expanse of Mecca, possessing nothing of her affair but a patience renewed each dawn. Here we should pause at the image as it is, without burdening it with commentary on the emotions, for the scene works on its own: a noble woman of the chiefs of Quraysh, sitting in the open for a year, awaiting a relief whose door she does not know.

Then relief came from the very place the affliction had come. A man of her cousins passed by her and felt pity for her, and said to the Banū al-Mughīra: "Will you not release this poor woman? You have separated her from her husband and her child!" So they said to her: "Join your husband if you wish." And the Banū ʿAbd al-Asad returned her son to her. This account is narrated in the Sīra of Ibn Isḥāq in the recension of Ibn Hishām, and the ḥāfiẓ Ibn Kathīr recorded it in al-Bidāya wa-l-Nihāya, and a number of scholars judged its chain to be sound (ḥasan).


## The Trusting Husband Who Knows His Wife's Worth

A reader may rush to blame Abū Salama: how could he go on to Medina and leave his wife and child in the grip of the people? Yet reflection on the context of the Hijra reveals a deeper meaning. His going was not abandonment; it was compliance with the command of emigration once every door before him had been shut, and a trust in a woman whose mettle he knew.

The trusting husband knows his wife's value, and her capacity — of soul and of mind — to guard herself and his honor, even when trials surround her and the days assail her. Abū Salama was not guarding his wife with his body; he had been kept from her by force. Rather, he was at peace knowing that she guarded herself by her religion and her reason. This is a lofty kind of trust, attained only by a husband who built his home upon respect for his partner rather than guardianship over her, and upon certainty that she was worthy of the trust of honor just as she was worthy of the trust of religion.

In answer to this trust, Umm Salama took the stand of one who does not disappoint her husband's expectation: a full year, alone and broken, with no certainty shaken in her, and no scarcity of helpers to weaken her patience. And when the door to joining him was opened, she did not hesitate; she set out to cross the desert alone with her little child. The woman who endures a year and then mounts into the unknown alone with such steadfastness affirms, by her very deed, everything her husband had trusted in her.

In this there is a correction of a widespread notion that reduces the safeguarding of a woman to walls and guards. The greatest thing that protects a woman is what she carries within herself of religion, reason, and chastity — not the restraints of men that surround her. Abū Salama understood this, and so he did not build his peace of mind upon holding her reins, but upon his knowledge of her mettle. This is the difference between a husband who guards his wife because he does not trust her, and a husband who is at peace with her because he knows she is too honorable in her own eyes to be debased. The first possesses a body and loses a heart; the second possesses the heart, and that suffices him over any guard.


## The Road-Companion Whom God Sent

Umm Salama set out from Mecca on her camel, her son with her and no third, bound for Medina across days of travel through a land in which no traveler was safe — let alone a woman alone with a child. When she reached al-Tanʿīm, ʿUthmān ibn Ṭalḥa met her — and he was, on that day, an idolater who had not yet entered Islam. He said: "Where to, daughter of Abū Umayya?" She said: "I want my husband in Medina." He said: "And is no one with you?" She said: "No, by God, except God and this little son of mine." He said: "By God, you must not be left."

So he took the halter of her camel and went on, leading her. She said, may God be pleased with her: "By God, I never accompanied any man of the Arabs whom I considered more honorable than he. When he reached a halting-place he would make my camel kneel for me, then withdraw away from me; when I had dismounted he would draw back from my camel and unload it, then lead it to a tree and tether it, then go off to another tree and lie down beneath it. When the time to travel drew near he would rise to my camel and bring it forward and saddle it, then withdraw away from me and say: 'Mount.' When I had mounted and settled on my camel, he would come and take its halter and lead it, until he brought me down." So he continued until he brought her to Qubāʾ, then said: "Your husband is in this village; enter it with the blessing of God." Then he turned back to Mecca.

Reflect on this scene in the light of the meaning of sitr. A believing woman, in the weakest state a human can pass through: alone, a stranger, with no human protector. So what did God do? He brought her a guardian from where she did not reckon — indeed from the very opposing camp: an idolater of Quraysh, in whom God harnessed chivalry and made it a shelter over His servant. He did not draw near, did not speak idly, did not let her dismount or mount while he looked on; he would lead the camel and then withdraw, make it kneel and then move off, until he delivered her to her place of safety and returned. This is the shelter God promised His friends: the servant need not possess the means of his own affair — it is enough that he be true, and God takes charge of arranging it and musters His soldiers for him: *And to God belong the soldiers of the heavens and the earth* [al-Fatḥ 48:7].

Add to this the completion of the circle: this man who guarded a believing woman's honor while still upon idolatry — God ennobled him years later, and he embraced Islam, and on the Day of Conquest the Prophet ﷺ returned to him the key of the Kaʿba and its custodianship, saying to the Banū Ṭalḥa: "Take it, an enduring inheritance; none shall wrest it from you but a wrongdoer." So whoever guards the shelter of a bondwoman for God's sake, God makes him a keeper of His Sacred House. The recompense is of the same kind as the deed.


## A Place for Comparison

How greatly we need to carry this household from the pages of the biographies into our own reality. We live in an age abounding in outward means of protection — instruments of communication, devices of monitoring, locks upon doors — and yet it is, withal, an age that complains of a strange fragility in marital trust. Many a home today collapses not under the blows of poverty, exile, or persecution, but under the weight of suspicion, mutual surveillance, and the absence of any certainty that one's partner is worthy of his or her trust.

Here the Prophetic example speaks its lesson: Abū Salama's home was torn apart by the days in a material rending of which our homes have not known a tenth — a year of separation, a child with a dislocated arm, a desert crossed alone — and yet the household emerged from the trial stronger than it had entered. Why? Because its support was not the nearness of bodies, but the trust of hearts and the truthful taking of refuge in God. The husband trusted and did not demean; the wife guarded and did not disappoint; and God sheltered and did not expose. How far is this from homes that possess every means of nearness yet lack the very root of trust?

Moreover, the story holds a lesson for the Muslim woman herself no less than the lesson for the man. Umm Salama was not a passive object awaiting a rescuer; she was an enduring agent: she was patient when nothing but patience was in her power, she moved when the door opened, and she mounted into the unknown with trust in her Lord — not in a companion or a guard. The woman who carries this certainty within herself is the one whose husband deserves to trust her, and the one whom God takes charge of sheltering. Shelter is sought through truthfulness; it is not attained through helplessness and dependence.

Shelter, in its reality, is not a lock upon a door, but the fruit of truthfulness with God. Whoever is true with God, He shelters him even in the bareness of the desert; and whoever betrays the root of the trust, He exposes him even behind seven doors.


## Further Signs of This Household's Honor

The honor of this household was not a passing moment on the road of the Hijra; it was an extended shade that followed it throughout its life.

Among that is that when Abū Salama died in Medina — from a wound he received on the Day of Uḥud that later reopened — Umm Salama grieved for him as one grieves for a home that was the first to emigrate. She spoke her famous words, relating what she had heard from him: "I heard God's Messenger ﷺ say: 'There is no Muslim who is struck by an affliction and says what God commanded — *Indeed we belong to God, and to Him we shall return* — "O God, reward me in my affliction and replace it for me with something better than it," except that God replaces it for him with something better than it.'" Muslim recorded it in his Ṣaḥīḥ (918). She said: "When Abū Salama died, I said it, then I said to myself: And who is better than Abū Salama? Yet God replaced him for me with His Messenger ﷺ." So the Prophet ﷺ married her, and she became a Mother of the Believers. What honor for a household whose compensation for the loss of its master was the Messenger of God himself? That is the most complete fulfillment of his words: "and replace it for me with something better than it."

And among the signs of her honor and her intellect — which affirms the trust her first husband placed in her — is her stand on the Day of Ḥudaybiya. When the Prophet ﷺ commanded his Companions to sacrifice and to shave, and they hesitated under the weight of what had befallen them, he entered upon Umm Salama distressed, and she counseled him with a sound opinion: that he go out and speak to no one until he had sacrificed his offering and called his barber to shave him, so that the people would do as he did. It was as she said: the people rose to sacrifice, and some began shaving others. Al-Bukhārī recorded it in his Ṣaḥīḥ (2731). So this woman whose intellect Abū Salama trusted in the guarding of his honor is the very one whose counsel the Prophet ﷺ trusted in the most critical situation the community faced. Her husband's trust in her was nothing if not well placed.


## The Harvest

You leave this household with two harvests, no Muslim able to do without either. The first is that the Hijra is a sacrifice in religion for which souls and families are given, and that whoever is true in it does not lose — rather God compensates him with something better than what he left. The second is that this household is a model of the stable marriage that the days and the winds of hardship do not shake, because it was founded on mutual trust: a trusting husband who knows his wife's worth, a safeguarding wife who does not disappoint his expectation, and above them both a Lord who shelters His servants and harnesses for them the soldiers of the heavens and the earth — until He makes the idolater a guard of the believer's honor, then rewards him for it by making him the custodian of His House.

So whoever desires God's shelter, let him be true with Him; and whoever desires a home that the storms cannot demolish, let him build it upon trust and not upon suspicion. What God has undertaken to shelter, none shall expose; and what is built upon the fear of God, the days shall not demolish.

And God knows best; He is the Granter of success, there is no Lord but He.
