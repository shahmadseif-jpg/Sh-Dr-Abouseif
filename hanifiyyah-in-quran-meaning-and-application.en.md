*A Qurʾanic study tracing the term "ḥanīf" across its every occurrence, from its linguistic root to its objective-based reading. By Dr. Ahmed Muhammad Ali Abouseif, President of the American Imams Academy (AIA).*

"Ḥanīfiyyah" — a term around which commentary and opinion have multiplied: clarifying its meaning at one turn, unfolding its implications at another, tracing it back to its linguistic root at a third — until the reader supposes he has grasped it fully from sheer repetition, while in truth he still stands at its edges. The word recurs in the Qurʾan twelve times: eight bound to the name of Abraham alone, two addressing the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ directly without mention of Abraham, and two final instances that widen to embrace the whole community of believers. This disparity in number and address is itself the first thing worth tracing, before we ask about meaning, evidence, purpose, and the character of those who seek it today.

### First: Counting the Word in the Qurʾan — Singular and Plural

The one who examines the Qurʾan finds that the word occurs in two forms only, no third: "ḥanīfan" in the singular, and "ḥunafāʾ" in the plural — while the noun "ḥanīfiyyah" itself, as a technical term, never appears in the Qurʾanic text at all; it is a Prophetic expression later distilled from the Qurʾanic description. The singular form occurs in ten places, eight of which wind directly around Abraham's name or his creed: in the disputation of the People of the Book, when it was said, "Be Jews or Christians," the reply came decisive — "Say: Rather, the creed of Abraham, ḥanīfan" (al-Baqarah: 135); in settling the dispute over his affiliation, "Abraham was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was a ḥanīf, a Muslim" (Āl ʿImrān: 67); the command to follow his creed then recurs in three further places (Āl ʿImrān: 95; al-Nisāʾ: 125; al-Naḥl: 123); the Qurʾan describes his own pure declaration of monotheism in his own words — "Indeed, I have turned my face toward Him who created the heavens and the earth, ḥanīfan, and I am not of the polytheists" (al-Anʿām: 79); it describes his comprehensive state — "Indeed, Abraham was a nation, devoutly obedient to God, ḥanīfan" (al-Naḥl: 120); and it commands the Prophet to affiliate himself with his creed — "Say: Indeed, my Lord has guided me to a straight path, an upright religion, the creed of Abraham, ḥanīfan" (al-Anʿām: 161). As for the remaining two instances of the singular, they address the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ alone, without mention of Abraham: "So set your face toward the religion, ḥanīfan" (Yūnus: 105), and "So direct your face toward the religion, ḥanīfan — the fiṭrah of God upon which He created mankind" (al-Rūm: 30). As for the plural form, "ḥunafāʾ," it occurs in two places that name neither Abraham nor Muhammad, but address believers in general, those commanded to sincerity: "ḥunafāʾ to God, not associating anything with Him" (al-Ḥajj: 31), and "They were not commanded except to worship God, sincere to Him in religion, as ḥunafāʾ" (al-Bayyinah: 5).

This gradation is not incidental but a deliberate architecture: from one particular prophet, to a final prophet commanded to follow him, to an entire community commanded to embody the description. The attribute widens as a circle widens around a stone dropped into water — from a single center to a boundless circumference.

### Second: Who Was Described as Ḥanīf?

At the center of this circle stands one man alone: Abraham, peace be upon him, the original bearer of the description, whom no other prophet shared by name — not Noah, not Moses, not Jesus — a distinction tied to his standing as the wellspring of the creeds later contested among the religions. The circle then widens a step to include the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, not by name but by command; his following of the ḥanīfiyyah is a following of a prior origin, not an invention of something new. It widens a final step to include believers in general, in the plural form, so that the description passes from the individual prophet to the entire community.

History, too, records that on the eve of the Prophetic mission, the Arabian Peninsula knew a small number of seekers whose unease never settled upon idol-worship, nor found rest in Judaism or Christianity, and who came to be called "the ḥunafāʾ" — most prominent among them Quss ibn Sāʿidah al-Iyādī, and Zayd ibn ʿAmr ibn Nufayl, who went on searching until he died years before the mission, and of whom the Prophet ﷺ said after his death: "He will be raised on the Day of Resurrection as a nation unto himself" (reported by al-Bukhārī, in the chapter on the merits of the Anṣār). These individuals bear witness that a remnant of fiṭrah had not been extinguished in the Peninsula — a remnant that the very root of the word explains before history does.

### Third: The Meaning of the Term, and Why Islam Alone Claimed It

In its linguistic root, ḥanaf is an inclination from misguidance toward uprightness, in contrast to janaf, which is an inclination from uprightness toward misguidance — and this precise contrast between the two consonants reveals that the Arabs distinguished linguistically between two kinds of inclination, reserving praise for only one. The word's origin is a tender, sensory image: a man whose foot turns inward is called "aḥnaf" — whence the Companion Ṣakhr ibn Qays took his epithet — and this inclination of the foot was carried over, in a remarkable transfer, to an inclination of the heart: the ḥanīf is one who inclines away from false religions toward monotheism, an inclination that looks, to one accustomed to collective conformity, like crookedness — yet is, on the scale of truth, the straightest of paths.

One might ask here: why did the lexicographers choose the word "inclination" rather than "uprightness" directly, when the ḥanīf's very aim is uprightness itself? The answer, as the exegetes have subtly noted, is that shirk itself is an inclination away from the original fiṭrah; so when a person rises to counter this inclination with an opposing inclination toward monotheism, he becomes, as it were, inclined away from the inclined — two opposing inclinations whose resultant is uprightness. This is what Imam al-Marāghī approached in his commentary on the verse of al-Rūm, when he said: "ḥanīfan, from ḥanaf, which is inclination — so he is one who inclines from misguidance toward uprightness." And this is truer to the reality than an alternative account that makes ḥanaf identical to uprightness without the mediation of inclination; for "uprightness" alone carries within its structure no reference to what preceded it, while "inclination" carries within it the very history of the deviation it corrects — describing a movement of correction, not a state of stillness that follows it. Nor is the human being asked, by this description, to incline against his own nature — a question the tongue alone cannot answer, but the Sunnah must, as will follow.

This same inclination is what led Islam to claim the term exclusively, as God's saying declares: "Abraham was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but he was a ḥanīf, a Muslim" (Āl ʿImrān: 67). This verse was revealed while the People of the Book disputed over Abraham, each side pulling him toward its own tent, and the reply came decisive: he was not merely prior to them in history, but outside their very categories in essence. Notice the pairing of "ḥanīfan" with "Muslim" in a single description; the verse does not make ḥanīfiyyah a third creed added to what was contested, but makes it the very essence of Islam in its first origin, before the laws of the prophets branched from it and were then corrupted. This is a point of comparison worth dwelling upon at length: the nations that dispute today over "who owns the truth" or "who inherits the great symbols" repeat, without realizing it, the People of the Book's dispute over Abraham, as each faction seeks to annex the great symbol to its own tent to gain historical legitimacy. The Qurʾan settles this dispute not by adding a third tent, but by raising Abraham above all the contending tents to a station that cannot be measured by tribal affiliation, but only by purity of heartfelt direction alone. It is for this reason that the naming of "Muslims" was likewise bound to Abraham's creed in the same context: "the creed of your father Abraham; it is He who named you Muslims" (al-Ḥajj: 78) — though most exegetes, such as al-Ṭabarī, hold that the pronoun "He" refers to God, not to Abraham. Yet on either reading, the naming remains inseparable from Abraham's creed, and precedes Muhammad ﷺ in both cases.

### Fourth: The Witness of the Sunnah to Ḥanīfiyyah, and the Determination of Its Meaning

Nor is the human being asked to incline against his own nature — and this is precisely what the Sunnah reveals when it fixes the meaning through three hadiths, each completing the others, none separable from the rest. The first is the hadith of fiṭrah: "Every child is born upon the fiṭrah; it is his parents who make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Zoroastrian"[^1] — which directly explains the verse of al-Rūm, "the fiṭrah of God upon which He created mankind," establishing that ḥanīfiyyah is an innate origin prior to upbringing, not something newly acquired; the inclination the tongue described is not a struggle against nature, but an agreement with it, for it is shirk that is the intruder, not monotheism. The second is the hadith of the natural practices of fiṭrah: "Five things belong to the fiṭrah: circumcision, shaving the pubic hair, trimming the mustache, clipping the nails, and plucking the armpit hair"[^2] — which brings fiṭrah down from abstraction to tangible practice, so that ḥanīfiyyah does not remain a suspended belief with no trace in the body or in habit. The third is the pair of hadiths on "the tolerant ḥanīfiyyah," which close the circle: "I was sent with the tolerant ḥanīfiyyah"[^3], and "The religion most beloved to God is the tolerant ḥanīfiyyah"[^4]. Ḥanīfiyyah, then, is an innate origin, with practical evidence, bound to tolerance rather than severity — three conditions by which the word cannot be understood if any is missing.

### Fifth: The Sayings of the Scholars in Light of the Objective-Based Vision

Upon these three conditions — fiṭrah, practical evidence, and tolerance — the scholars of the higher objectives (maqāṣid) built their reading of ḥanīfiyyah, foremost among them Ibn ʿĀshūr, who counted tolerance, in *Maqāṣid al-Sharīʿah al-Islāmiyyah*, as "the foremost attribute of this Sharīʿah and its greatest objective," and defined it as "ease of dealing in matters where people are accustomed to contention" — a mean between severity and laxity, favoring neither extreme. He explained the wisdom of tolerance's pairing with ḥanīfiyyah by noting that the Sharīʿah is "the religion of fiṭrah," and that matters of fiṭrah return to an innate disposition already present within souls, so that their acceptance comes easily — unlike a duty that were foreign to the very nature of creation. The inclination, in his view — as we have seen both in the word's linguistic root and in the hadith together — is an inclination that agrees with innate disposition, not one that opposes it.

As for Ibn Taymiyyah, in *Iqtiḍāʾ al-Ṣirāṭ al-Mustaqīm li-Mukhālafat Aṣḥāb al-Jaḥīm*, he tied ḥanīfiyyah to a different objective: the preservation of identity and distinctiveness, holding that differing from other religious communities in outward practice is part of the meaning of ḥanīfiyyah — not because such difference is sought for its own sake, but because imitation dissolves, step by step, the distinguishing marks between pure monotheism and whatever deviation has mingled with it. In the same vein, Ibn al-Qayyim, in *Miftāḥ Dār al-Saʿādah*, treated fiṭrah and ḥanīfiyyah as two faces of a single reality: that the soul, so long as it is not diverted from its origin by some external influence, inclines of itself to acknowledge the one Creator — monotheism, in his view, being a retrieval of what already exists within the self, not a planting from without; a view that meets, from an objective-based angle, what Ibn ʿĀshūr established regarding tolerance as ease — for it, too, agrees with innate disposition rather than opposing it.

### Sixth: The Character of Those Who Seek Ḥanīfiyyah

From these readings taken together emerges a portrait of the true seeker of ḥanīfiyyah, no part of which stands apart from the rest. He is, first of all, an active seeker, not a passive claimant of affiliation; for the historical ḥunafāʾ did not wait for truth — they went out asking after it until they found it. His inclination is sound only through a sincerity that prevents him from taking ḥanīfiyyah as a name without substance, as in "sincere to Him in religion, as ḥunafāʾ" (al-Bayyinah: 5). Nor does his sincerity stop at the boundary of belief; it widens, just as Abraham's own direction widened when he said, "I have turned my face," to encompass every avenue of life, not one rite alone. At the same time, he does not confuse the severity owed to the root of belief with the tolerance owed to the branches of dealing — growing harsh in matters of monotheism while lax with himself in dealings, or doing the reverse and losing the balance either way. And what distinguishes him last is a steadfastness before the pressure of inherited custom, just as Abraham parted from his father and his people in pursuit of truth; ḥanīfiyyah, for him, is a trial faced anew in every generation, not a description inherited automatically.

### Conclusion

After this full circuit, it becomes clear that the multiplication of sayings around ḥanīfiyyah was not a proliferation that lost the meaning, but a bringing of it into clearer view from every side. And we have seen, in the very root of the language, why this inclination — rather than uprightness alone — was the more precise description: because it carries within it the history of the deviation it corrects, not the stillness that follows correction. What remains, at the core of it all, however many its facets, is one thing undivided: that monotheism is a continuous inclination toward uprightness, never a station reached where its seeker comes to rest.

And God knows best; He is the Grantor of success; there is no lord but Him.
