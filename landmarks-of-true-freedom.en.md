# The Landmarks of True Freedom in the Noble Qurʾan
## When the Human Being Is Liberated from Creation toward the Creator
### From the Emancipation of the Individual Heart to the Establishment of a Free Civilization

---

It was said of old in defining the free person: "Truly free is he who has no grace from anyone to be repaid" — making the essence of freedom the independence of the will from the favors of creation, and the disentanglement of the heart from the bondage of hope and fear directed at people. This is a noble meaning that approaches the Qurʾanic truth and stands on its threshold; yet the Qurʾan raises it to its highest aim: that the heart be attached to God alone, leaving no room for attachment to anything else.

Freedom in the scales of revelation is not merely emancipation from legal bondage, nor is it reducible to a political meaning. It is, first and foremost, the liberation of the inner self from the authority of human beings, from ego and greed and fear and desire — from everything that enslaves the heart except God. The free in the Qurʾan are not a social class; they are a people whose hearts have been liberated from every authority except the authority of God. This is the unifying thread running through the landmarks of this study, and the supreme principle to which it returns: the more the heart attaches itself to God, the more it is freed from all else.

---

## First: The Models of the Free — When Freedom Becomes Embodied in People

The Qurʾan did not leave freedom as an abstract idea floating in theory; it fashioned it into living stances in souls that proved their sincerity, so that when the reader beheld the portrait of a free person, they knew freedom without need of a definition. These are its most prominent landmarks in the lives of people:

### 1 — Abū Bakr al-Ṣiddīq: The Free One Who Cannot Be Bought

The verses of Sūrat al-Layl descended about him as a constitution of freedom from the servitude of wealth and obligation:

﴾And the most righteous will be kept away from it — he who gives his wealth to purify himself, and has no favor owed to him by anyone, seeking only the countenance of his Lord Most High﴿ [al-Layl: 17–20]

He used to purchase the persecuted — like Bilāl ibn Rabāḥ — and free them. His father said to him: "I see you freeing weak necks; why not free men of strength who could protect you?" He replied: "I want only what is with God." He is free because he does not perform goodness to purchase loyalty, nor to repay a prior favor, nor to build a network of interests. His only gate is ﴾seeking the countenance of his Lord Most High﴿. He did not free Bilāl so that Bilāl would become his servant — but so that he would be God's servant alone. So the freeing was a double freeing: the freeing of a neck from bondage, and the freeing of a giver from the bondage of obligation.

### 2 — The Sorcerers of Pharaoh: Freedom in an Hour of Certainty

Moments earlier they had been mercenaries begging for wages and rank ﴾Is there for us a reward?﴿ — then, when their hearts were illumined, they were emancipated in a single breath, confronting the tyrant with the most elevated words ever spoken by a free person:

﴾We will never prefer you over what has come to us of clear proofs and over He who created us. So decree whatever you are going to decree. You can only decree for this worldly life.﴿ [Tāhā: 72]

This is among the greatest verses of freedom in the Qurʾan. When they came to know that Pharaoh possessed nothing but their perishable bodies ﴾you can only decree for this worldly life﴿, their souls were emancipated, and his threats of crucifixion and cutting off of hands lost all value. Freedom here is the child of certainty — the prestige of the ruler was shattered the moment the fear of death collapsed.

### 3 — The Believer from the Family of Pharaoh: Freedom in Speaking Truth to Power

A man who concealed his faith in the tyrant's palace — his attachment to his position and his proximity to power did not prevent him from speaking out when he saw the conspiracy to kill Moses:

﴾Will you kill a man because he says, "My Lord is God"?﴿ [Ghāfir: 28]

He was emancipated from fear for his influence and standing inside the palace, placing the truth of principle above the safety of the body. He is the model of the free person who confronts tyranny with argument from within its own court.

### 4 — Joseph, upon him be peace: The Freedom of Chastity and Self-Elevation

He was bought and sold in the slave market, then faced the enticement of the master's wife, her authority, and her threat of imprisonment — and he chose the narrowness of the prison over the spaciousness of the palace with the servitude of desire:

﴾He said: My Lord, prison is more beloved to me than what they invite me to.﴿ [Yūsuf: 33]

For a person may be inside prison and yet be free, and may be outside it and yet a slave to their desires. Joseph chose the chains of walls over the chains of desire, and so was truly free even as he was shackled by the bonds of apparent bondage.

### 5 — Abraham, upon him be peace: Liberation from the Servitude of the Familiar

He confronted his people and his father alone, shattering their idols — both tangible and conceptual — liberating himself from the heaviest shackle that binds minds: the shackle of blind deference to fathers, majorities, and inherited tradition:

﴾What are these statues to which you are devoted? They said: We found our fathers worshipping them. He said: You and your fathers have been in clear error.﴿ [al-Anbiāʾ: 52–54]

Then his freedom reached its apex in the fire — for his heart had grown cool through faith before the fire cooled by God's command ﴾O fire, be coolness and peace﴿, and the fire was left with no authority over his decision.

### 6 — Moses, upon him be peace: Liberation from Fear through Trust in God

When his people saw the sea before them and the enemy behind them and said ﴾We are surely overtaken﴿, Moses saw what they did not see — he saw God — and spoke at the highest station of liberation from fear:

﴾No! Indeed, with me is my Lord; He will guide me.﴿ [al-Shuʾarāʾ: 62]

### 7 — Ḥabīb al-Najjār: The Freedom of Giving Without Return

He came running from the farthest part of the city to counsel his people, and he set out the measure of freedom clearly in his description of the messengers:

﴾Follow those who do not ask of you any payment, and they are rightly guided.﴿ [Yāsīn: 21]

The sign of their truthfulness and their freedom is that they seek no payment and no obligation upon anyone. Then he himself gave the finest example when they killed him — he bore no grudge but wished them guidance: ﴾O, I wish my people could know.﴿

### 8 — Āsiyah, Women, and the Youth: The Freedom of Conscience

The Qurʾan contains other landmarks raising the banner of freedom: Āsiyah, the wife of Pharaoh — a free heart in an enslaved household ﴾My Lord, build for me near You a house in Paradise and save me from Pharaoh﴿. The Companions of the Ditch whose beliefs the fire could not purchase ﴾And they resented them for nothing except that they believed in God﴿. The Young Men of the Cave who abandoned homeland and rank and preserved what is most precious to possess ﴾And We bound their hearts together﴿. The believers at the siege who were not enslaved by the blockade ﴾And it increased them only in faith and submission﴿. These are all the free of conscience — their bodies were seized, but their hearts were not.

## Second: The Foundations of Freedom — When the Verses Establish It as a Principle

Just as the Qurʾan embodied freedom in people, it established it as universal foundations in the construction of moral obligation — so it remains not merely an individual stance but becomes a universal law:

The foundation of creedal freedom is established as legislation: ﴾There is no compulsion in religion. The right course has become clear from error.﴿ [al-Baqara: 256] — a liberation of the mind to see the truth clearly and choose it by the fullness of its will. Freedom was paired with responsibility: ﴾And say: The truth is from your Lord, so whoever wills — let him believe; and whoever wills — let him disbelieve.﴿ [al-Kahf: 29] — the human being is free but responsible for their choice. God drew the parable of the owned slave against the free man who spends freely in Sūrat al-Naḥl [75], to clarify that the truly free person is he who has liberated himself from the servitude of wealth and placed it in obedience to God. And the foundation of foundations: the liberation of the self from its own desires ﴾But as for he who feared the position of his Lord and restrained the soul from desire﴿ [al-Nāziʾat: 40].

### A Comprehensive Principle: Recompense from God Alone

There is a principle that completes and crowns the landmark of "liberation from obligation": that the free person receives their recompense from God alone — they are not enslaved by awaiting payment from creation, nor do they need people to witness their deeds. God said: ﴾And whatever good you spend will be fully repaid to you, and you will not be wronged.﴿ [al-Baqara: 272] And He said: ﴾And whatever good you do — God knows it.﴿ [al-Baqara: 197] These two verses liberate the giver from two hidden chains: the chain of awaiting recompense from the recipient, and the chain of seeking the praise and notice of people. Since God knows the good and repays it in full, the free person has no need for an obligation to be returned or praise to be sought. This is precisely what was established in the case of Abū Bakr ﴾And has no favor owed to him by anyone, seeking only the countenance of his Lord Most High﴿ — when all recompense becomes suspended from God, the heart is emancipated from the bondage of everything else.

These landmarks are gathered into six foundations of Qurʾanic freedom: liberation from fear ﴾Decree whatever you are going to decree﴿, from greed and obligation ﴾Has no favor owed by anyone﴿, from desire ﴾Prison is more beloved to me﴿, from social pressure (the stance of Abraham), from the authority of the ruler (Moses, the sorcerers, and the believer from Pharaoh's family), and from the servitude of the self ﴾And restrained the soul from desire﴿.

## Third: From the Emancipation of the Heart to the Liberation of the Nation

The Qurʾan does not leave individual liberation suspended in a void; it builds the bridge from it to civilization, making freedom a universal law in the building of nations — not merely the story of individuals. The links in this are connected:

### 1 — The Free Individual as the Seed of the Free Nation

The Qurʾan begins change from the heart, not from the structure: ﴾Indeed, God will not change the condition of a people until they change what is in themselves.﴿ [al-Raʾd: 11] Social enslavement is not lifted by a revolution against the tyrant first, but by the emancipation of the souls that consented to enslavement. The first thing the Qurʾan liberates is the interior — then freedom flows from it outward.

### 2 — Diagnosing the Mechanisms of Enslavement

The Qurʾan diagnosed the instrument of social enslavement in three things: trivialization ﴾And he belittled his people, and they obeyed him﴿ [al-Zukhruf: 54] — the tyrant first empties the collective mind. Division ﴾He made its people into factions, oppressing a sector of them﴿ [al-Qaṣaṣ: 4] — for fragmentation is the precondition of subjugation. And acceptance of humiliation — for in His words ﴾and they obeyed him﴿ there is a sharp commentary: enslavement is a partnership between an arrogant oppressor and a willing victim. Hence the angels rebuked those who remained behind ﴾Was not the earth of God spacious enough for you to emigrate within it?﴿ [al-Nisāʾ: 97] — meaning that freedom is a responsibility, and one who abandons it is not excused by the claim of weakness alone.

### 3 — The Mission of the Prophets as a Collective Liberation Project

The mission of revelation at its core is the breaking of collective chains: ﴾And removes from them their burden and the shackles which were upon them.﴿ [al-Aʾrāf: 157] The free person — the prophet, the reformer — does not content himself with his own salvation but is directed toward liberating his people ﴾Go to Pharaoh﴿ ﴾And send with us the Children of Israel﴿. Moses's individual liberation ﴾Indeed, with me is my Lord﴿ became a bridge to the liberation of an entire nation from servitude.

### 4 — The Rites as a School of Collective Liberation

The Qurʾan transformed freedom from an individual stance into a recurring social training: the testimony of monotheism is itself a declaration of collective emancipation — "There is no god" is a negation of every authority; "except God" is the affirmation of a single authority. The row in prayer dissolves the distinctions upon which class-based enslavement rests, and the Hajj strips away the symbols of distinction by which people enslave people.

### 5 — The Comprehensive Law: The Freedom of Hearts as the Foundation of Civilization

Here the two keys converge: just as the individual heart, the more it attaches to God, is freed from all else — so it is with the nation. No free nation stands except upon free hearts. Therefore the Qurʾan linked the righteousness of the community to an inner moral principle: ﴾Those who, if We give them authority in the land, establish prayer.﴿ [al-Ḥajj: 41] — for empowerment — which is political freedom — is the fruit of sincere servitude to God — which is spiritual freedom — not the reverse.

---

## Conclusion: Freedom Is a Fruit, Not a Gift

Freedom in the Qurʾan is a chain of connected links: emancipation of the heart from creation in the individual models → formation of a free believing nucleus → confronting the three mechanisms of enslavement → liberation of the nation through mission and rites → establishment of a free civilization that upholds justice. The comprehensive principle for both ends is one: freedom is not a gift bestowed by a ruler, but a fruit that grows from sincere servitude to God — whether individual or collective.

With this, the definition of the free person with which we opened this article comes into alignment and reaches its Qurʾanic aim: the truly free is not only he who has no favor owed by anyone — but he whose heart is so filled with God that no room remains in it for the authority of anything else. And that is the freedom that no prison weakens, no power purchases, no fire extinguishes.

As we opened with the verse of al-Layl, we close with it, so that the circle seals at its beginning: he who made his giving ﴾seeking only the countenance of his Lord Most High﴿, seeking from creation neither obligation nor recompense — his freedom bears this fruit: that satisfaction is not granted him nor given him except by God. So the verse ends with a promise held by none other: ﴾And he shall be well-pleased.﴿ He who was liberated from seeking payment among people — God undertook his satisfaction alone; for full satisfaction never reaches the servant from a creature's direction, but is the gift of the Creator to him who dedicated his face to Him alone. That is the goal of the free person and their end: to be liberated from all, to win the satisfaction of the One — to be free from everyone, bound only to the satisfaction of God. And that is the bondage whose very essence is emancipation, and the servitude that is the very sum of freedom.
